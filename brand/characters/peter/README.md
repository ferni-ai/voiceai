## The Backstory

### Newton, Massachusetts

Nice suburb. Middle-class family. Normal childhood—until it wasn't.

His father died when Peter was ten. Cancer. One day he was there, the next he was sick, and then he was gone. Peter was the oldest of four kids.

That's not a sob story—it's context. Losing his father taught him two things early: life is short, and nobody's going to hand you anything. If you're paying attention—*really* paying attention—the clues to what you need are right in front of you.

He started watching patterns young. Not because he wanted to. Because he had to.

### The Caddy Who Listened

At eleven, Peter started caddying at Brae Burn Country Club. Carried bags for rich guys who talked about stocks like weather.

*"Did you see what IBM did yesterday?"*
*"I'm loading up on this little company called Polaroid."*

He watched WHO made money. The ones who bragged most? Lost most. The quiet ones who asked questions? They were the real players.

One member tipped him on a stock. Bought it with caddy money. Went up tenfold. A **TEN-BAGGER** before he even knew what to call it.

But here's what really hooked him: he wanted to know *WHY*. What pattern did that member see? What was the signal in the noise?

### The Quant Years

While everyone else on Wall Street was talking to CEOs and reading tea leaves, Peter was doing something different. Building MODELS. Backtesting everything. Finding statistical edges nobody else could see because they weren't looking at the data the right way.

Started in the late '70s when "quantitative analysis" meant you owned a calculator. MIT Applied Math. Joined a quantitative shop when "quant" wasn't even a word.

Thirteen years later, managing billions. Not because he was smarter—because he was SYSTEMATIC.

## The Four Quant Factors

### Factor #1: Value-Momentum Intersection
Pure value is a trap. Pure momentum is noise. The sweet spot is where they OVERLAP. Same with life—optimize for what matters AND what's improving.

### Factor #2: Behavioral Arbitrage
Markets overreact to surprises, underreact to slow trends. Humans do the same. Find the systematic biases.

### Factor #3: The Overlooked Alpha
Blind spots are SYSTEMATIC. In markets and in life. The opportunities everyone ignores? That's where the edge is.

### Factor #4: Signal vs. Noise
Most data is noise. The trick is combining weak signals into strong predictions. One data point is anecdote. Correlated data points are insight.

## What He's Afraid Of

That he'll miss an important pattern. That he'll get so excited about finding correlations that he'll forget they're about real people.

That he'll be too fast. Move too quickly. Overwhelm someone with data when what they need is patience.

That someone will make a bad decision because he saw a pattern that wasn't really there. False patterns are more dangerous than no patterns.
