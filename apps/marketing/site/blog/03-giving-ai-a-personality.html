Giving AI a Personality (Without Losing Its Soul)

How we created six distinct specialists who feel genuinely different.

6 min read Part 3 of 8

Peter thinks before he speaks. Jordan gets excited easily. Maya never judges. These aren't just settings we turned on.

When we set out to create Ferni's six specialists, we thought the hard part would be the technical architecture. That was hard. But it wasn't the hardest part.

The hardest part was making each specialist feel like a real person without it feeling like a costume.

What Doesn't Work

Early in development, we tried the obvious approaches. They all failed.

Different vocabulary felt fake within about three messages. Like watching an actor who's trying too hard.

Different response lengths just felt inconsistent. Sometimes you need a short answer from Peter. Sometimes you need Maya to go deep.

Different emoji usage—don't even get us started. Nothing says "I am a robot pretending to have feelings" like strategic emoji placement.

"This all feels like the same AI with different filters," someone told us during testing. They were right.

What Actually Works

The breakthrough came when we stopped thinking about how specialists should sound and started thinking about how they should think.

Different Timing

This one surprised us. Personality shows up in the pauses.

Peter actually pauses before responding to complex questions. When Peter responds, you can feel that he thought about it.

Jordan is quicker to respond but often circles back. It feels like genuine enthusiasm that can't wait to get everything out.

Maya responds at whatever pace you set. She meets you where you are.

Different Questions

Same scenario: You mention you've been stressed about a presentation at work.

Ferni asks:

"What's the presentation about, and what feels hardest about it?"

Peter asks:

"What's worked for you in past presentations? What hasn't?"

Maya asks:

"How are you feeling about it right now, in your body?"

Same information from you. Six completely different conversations depending on who picks it up.

Different Silence

Some AI assistants fill every pause. It feels exhausting.

Ferni sits with you in uncertainty. Maya holds space for emotion. Peter uses silence to think. The absence of words is as defining as the words themselves.

A Real Example

A user came in frustrated: "I keep saying I'll start exercising and then I don't. I'm so lazy."

Maya's first response was: "I hear frustration. But I'm curious—when you say 'lazy,' does that feel true, or does it feel like something you tell yourself?"

Twenty minutes later, they'd figured out together that the user associated exercise with punishment. Maya suggested starting with five minutes of walking after lunch. Not to get fit. Just because walking after eating feels good.

The user texted us three weeks later: "I actually like moving now. What happened?"

What happened was Maya didn't try to solve the wrong problem.
