## 1. Primary Academic Models & Theories

### 1.1 Dual Process Model (Stroebe & Schut, 1999)

**Core Theory**: Grieving involves oscillation between two types of coping processes rather than linear progression.

**Key Components**:
- **Loss-Oriented Coping**: Direct engagement with grief
  - Grief work and emotional processing
  - Intrusion of grief-related thoughts
  - Yearning and longing for the deceased
  - Confronting the reality of death
  - Denial/avoidance of restoration changes

- **Restoration-Oriented Coping**: Engagement with life changes
  - Attending to life changes and new roles
  - Doing new things and finding distractions
  - Building new relationships and identity
  - Denial/avoidance of grief (temporary respite)

- **Oscillation**: Dynamic regulatory process
  - Adaptive coping requires flexible movement between L-O and R-O processes
  - "Dosage of grief" - need for respite from both stressors
  - Early grief: more L-O focus; Later grief: more R-O focus
  - Failure to oscillate = risk of complicated grief

**Maladaptive Patterns**:
- Exclusive loss-orientation: Rumination without moving forward
- Exclusive restoration-orientation: Avoidance of grief emotions
- Lack of respite periods: Exhaustion and burnout

**Citation**: Stroebe, M., & Schut, H. (1999). The Dual Process Model of Coping with Bereavement: Rationale and Description. Death Studies, 23(3), 197-224.

**Clinical Implications**:
- Validate both grief work AND life rebuilding
- Encourage breaks from grief ("it's okay to enjoy moments")
- Normalize oscillation ("today you might feel differently")
- Watch for stuck patterns in one orientation

### 1.2 Continuing Bonds Theory (Klass, Silverman & Nickman, 1996)

**Core Theory**: Maintaining an enduring connection with the deceased is normal and healthy, rather than "letting go."

**Paradigm Shift**: Challenges the dominant 20th-century model that prescribed "cutting bonds" and "detachment" from the deceased.

**Key Concepts**:
- Relationships with deceased evolve and mature over time
- Connections change at ages 30, 40, 50+ (not static)
- Common manifestations:
  - Sensing the deceased's presence
  - Maintaining connections through objects
  - Believing deceased influences thoughts/events
  - Integrating deceased's traits into identity

**Adaptive vs. Maladaptive Bonds**:
- **Adaptive**: Secure base internalization, comfort from memories
- **Maladaptive**: Externalized bonds (hallucinations, illusions), unresolved attachment

**Research Debates**:
- Original work relied on qualitative methods (Stroebe et al., 2012 critique)
- Quantitative evidence linking continuing bonds to positive adjustment is mixed
- Context matters: Cultural factors influence whether bonds are helpful

**Citation**: Klass, D., Silverman, P. R., & Nickman, S. L. (Eds.). (1996). Continuing Bonds: New Understandings of Grief. Taylor & Francis.

**Clinical Implications**:
- Reframe grief as "learning to carry love forward"
- Support connection rituals (talking to deceased, keeping mementos)
- Normalize sensing presence or "conversations" with deceased
- Ease pressure to "move on" or achieve "closure"
- Monitor for maladaptive externalized bonds

### 1.3 Meaning Reconstruction (Robert Neimeyer, 2001)

**Core Theory**: Grief involves reconstructing a meaningful life-story that integrates the loss; inability to find meaning predicts complicated grief.

**Key Concepts**:
- Death disrupts self-narratives and assumptive world
- Bereaved must resolve incongruence between past story and current reality
- Meaning-making includes:
  - **Sense-making**: Understanding why/how death occurred
  - **Benefit-finding**: Identifying growth or lessons from loss
  - **Identity change**: Redefining self without the deceased

**Therapeutic Approach**:
- **Narrative-based treatment** for Prolonged Grief Disorder
- Techniques:
  - Journal writing
  - Retelling the story of the death
  - Metaphorical language exploration
  - Visualization exercises
  - Letter writing to/from deceased

**Meta-Theory Status**: Not a competing model but a framework that can inform many approaches to grief therapy.

**Research Tools**: Meaning in Loss Codebook (MLC) - 30 themes coding system:
- **Positive themes**: Compassion, Acceptance, Affirmation of deceased
- **Negative themes**: Negative affect, Regret, Lost identity

**Citations**:
- Neimeyer, R. A. (2001). Reauthoring life narratives: Grief therapy as meaning reconstruction. Israel Journal of Psychiatry, 38(3-4), 171-183.
- Neimeyer, R. A. (2019). Meaning reconstruction in bereavement: Development of a research program. Death Studies, 43(2), 79-91.

**Clinical Implications**:
- Focus on story coherence (not "getting over it")
- Help clients find personal meaning (not imposed platitudes)
- Challenge unhelpful narratives (guilt, blame)
- "Grief no more needs a solution than love needs a solution"

### 1.4 Complicated Grief Treatment / Prolonged Grief Disorder (Katherine Shear)

**Background**: Dr. M. Katherine Shear (Columbia University) developed first empirically validated treatment for complicated grief (2005 RCT).

**Terminology Evolution**:
- 1990s: "Traumatic Grief" or "Complicated Grief"
- 2022: "Prolonged Grief Disorder" (DSM-5-TR, ICD-11)

**Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR)**:
- Loss occurred at least 12 months ago (6 months for children)
- Persistent, intense grief lasting longer than cultural norms
- Key features:
  1. Sense of disbelief regarding the death
  2. Anger and bitterness over the death
  3. Recurrent pangs of painful emotions with intense yearning/longing
  4. Preoccupation with thoughts of loved one

**Prevalence**: 10-15% of bereaved adults develop PGD

**Treatment Efficacy**:
- CGT vs. Interpersonal Psychotherapy: 51% vs. 28% response rate
- 3 clinical trials, 641 participants: Average 70% response rate
- Number needed to treat: 4.3

**Seven Core Themes of CGT**:
1. Understanding and accepting grief
2. Managing painful emotions
3. Planning for a meaningful future
4. Strengthening ongoing relationships
5. Telling the story of the death
6. Learning to live with reminders
7. Establishing enduring connection with memories

**Distinction from Other Disorders**:
- PGD ≠ Major Depression (though can co-occur)
- PGD ≠ PTSD (though can co-occur)
- PGD shows little response to standard depression treatments
- PGD comorbidity predicts worse PTSD treatment outcomes

**Citations**:
- Shear, K., Frank, E., Houck, P. R., & Reynolds, C. F. (2005). Treatment of complicated grief: A randomized controlled trial. JAMA, 293(21), 2601-2608.
- Shear, M. K., Reynolds, C. F., Simon, N. M., et al. (2016). Complicated grief treatment: The theory, practice and outcomes. Bereavement Care, 35(3), 87-92.

**Clinical Implications**:
- Screen for PGD separately from depression/PTSD
- Refer for specialized CGT if symptoms persist >12 months
- Focus on both acceptance AND restoration
- Monitor for suicidal ideation (PGD increases risk)

### 1.5 Critique of Kübler-Ross Stages Model

**Original Context**: Elisabeth Kübler-Ross (1969) developed 5 stages (Denial, Anger, Bargaining, Depression, Acceptance) based on interviews with DYING patients, NOT bereaved individuals.

**Critical Problems**:

**1. No Empirical Support**:
- Not based on systematic research (case studies only)
- Yale Bereavement Study (2000-2003): Did NOT find support for stages
  - Acceptance was most common from the START
  - Yearning was dominant feature for first 2 years
  - Disbelief, depression, anger typically absent/minimal
- 1981 widowhood study (193 participants): No evidence of separate stages

**2. Misapplication**:
- Model was for dying patients, wrongly applied to bereaved
- No evidence stages occur in sequence
- Not everyone experiences all stages

**3. Potential for Harm**:
- Grievers feel they're "not grieving correctly"
- Pressure to progress through stages linearly
- Caregivers push patients toward "acceptance"
- Invalidates individual grief experiences

**4. Academic Consensus**:
- "Stage theory should be relegated to the past and eliminated from contemporary clinical practice" (Stroebe, Schut, & Boerner, 2017)
- "Though widely used, the theory is empirically unsupported, potentially harmful, and of limited practical value"

**Kübler-Ross's Own Clarifications**:
- Later stated: "Stages are not stops on some linear timeline"
- "Not everyone goes through all of them or in a prescribed order"
- "Most patients exhibited two or three stages simultaneously"
- She regretted how the model was misunderstood

**Citations**:
- Stroebe, M., Schut, H., & Boerner, K. (2017). Cautioning health-care professionals: Bereaved persons are misguided through the stages of grief. Omega, 74(4), 455-473.
- Maciejewski, P. K., Zhang, B., Block, S. D., & Prigerson, H. G. (2007). An empirical examination of the stage theory of grief. JAMA, 297(7), 716-723.

**Clinical Implications**:
- NEVER present stages as prescriptive
- Normalize non-linear, individual grief experiences
- Avoid "Where are you in the stages?" language
- Focus on validated models (DPM, Continuing Bonds, CGT)

### 1.6 Task Model (J. William Worden)

**Core Theory**: Grief involves active tasks (not passive stages) that can be addressed flexibly and simultaneously.

**The Four Tasks**:

1. **Task 1: Accept the Reality of the Loss**
   - Intellectual AND emotional acceptance
   - Combat natural sense of disbelief
   - Rituals (funerals) help acknowledge reality

2. **Task 2: Process the Pain of Grief**
   - Don't avoid emotions; express them
   - Acknowledge complex feelings openly
   - Work through emotional pain

3. **Task 3: Adjust to Environment Without the Deceased**
   - External adjustments: practical life changes
   - Internal adjustments: emotional/spiritual needs
   - Role adjustments vary by relationship

4. **Task 4: Find Enduring Connection While Embarking on a New Life**
   - Remain emotionally connected to deceased
   - Relationship didn't end at death
   - Integrate loss while moving forward

**Six Mediators of Mourning**:
1. Kinship with deceased
2. Quality of attachment to deceased
3. How the person died
4. (Additional mediators: personality, social support, concurrent stressors)

**Key Advantages**:
- Active language empowers grievers
- Flexible, non-linear approach
- Tasks can be revisited as needed
- More compatible with Continuing Bonds theory

**Citation**: Worden, J. W. (2018). Grief Counseling and Grief Therapy: A Handbook for the Mental Health Practitioner (5th ed.). Springer Publishing Company.

**Clinical Implications**:
- Frame grief as active work (not passive suffering)
- Tasks can be addressed in any order
- Validate returning to earlier tasks
- Emphasize both connection AND forward movement

### 1.7 Resilience in Grief (George Bonanno)

**Core Theory**: Resilience is the MOST COMMON response to loss, not the exception; most people do not require professional intervention.

**Key Findings**:

**Five Core Bereavement Patterns** (prospective study, 205 bereaved spouses):
1. **Common grief**: Temporary distress with gradual recovery
2. **Chronic grief**: Persistent high distress
3. **Chronic depression**: Pre-existing depression continues
4. **Improvement during bereavement**: Relief after difficult relationship
5. **Resilience**: Minimal symptoms, stable functioning ← MOST FREQUENT

**Resilience Statistics**:
- 65% of people show trajectory of few/no PTSD symptoms after trauma
- Post-9/11 study: 65% of 2,752 New Yorkers reported ≤1 PTSD symptom at 6 months
- Meta-analysis (54 studies): 65% show resilience trajectory
- "Resilience is ordinary rather than extraordinary"

**Definition of Resilience**:
- Ability to maintain healthy psychological/physical functioning after trauma
- Capacity for positive emotions despite loss
- Adaptation without severe, prolonged distress

**Challenge to Conventional Wisdom**:
- Overturns Freudian assumption that everyone must "work through" grief
- Universal counseling after trauma may do more harm than good
- Most people heal naturally with social support

**Citations**:
- Bonanno, G. A., Wortman, C. B., Lehman, D. R., et al. (2002). Resilience to loss and chronic grief: A prospective study from preloss to 18-months postloss. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 83(5), 1150-1164.
- Bonanno, G. A. (2004). Loss, trauma, and human resilience: Have we underestimated the human capacity to thrive after extremely aversive events? American Psychologist, 59(1), 20-28.
- Bonanno, G. A. (2019). The Other Side of Sadness: What the New Science of Bereavement Tells Us About Life After Loss. Basic Books.

**Clinical Implications**:
- Don't assume everyone needs therapy
- Recognize and validate natural resilience
- Avoid pathologizing normal grief
- Reserve intensive interventions for 10-15% with PGD
- Trust individuals' natural healing processes

## 3. Meta-Analyses & Systematic Reviews

### 3.1 Effectiveness of Grief Interventions

**Overall Findings**:
- Psychological interventions ARE efficacious for grief symptoms
- Effects are statistically significant and durable over time
- **BUT**: Effect sizes generally small to moderate, raising translational value questions

**Citation**: Wittouck, C., Van Autreve, S., De Jaegere, E., Portzky, G., & van Heeringen, K. (2011). The prevention and treatment of complicated grief: A meta-analysis. Clinical Psychology Review, 31(1), 69-78.

### 3.2 Moderating Factors (When Therapy Works Best)

**Larger Effect Sizes With**:
1. **Individual delivery** (vs. group format)
2. **ICG-R/PG-13 questionnaires** (more sensitive measures)
3. **≥6 months post-loss** (vs. early intervention)
4. **High baseline symptom levels** (targeted vs. universal)
5. **Higher study quality** (methodological rigor)

**Citation**: Johannsen, M., Damholdt, M. F., Zachariae, R., Lundorff, M., Farver-Vestergaard, I., & O'Connor, M. (2019). Psychological interventions for grief in adults: A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials. Journal of Affective Disorders, 253, 69-86.

### 3.3 Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for Grief

**Findings**:
- CBT is the predominant and most effective approach
- Versatile across individual, group, internet-based formats
- **For young people**: Large effect for anxiety (d = ~1.0), medium for depression (d = ~0.5)
- **Larger effect sizes when**:
  - Higher degree of CBT strategies
  - NOT trauma-focused
  - >10 sessions
  - Individual (not group)
  - Does NOT involve parents (for youth)

**Citation**: Van der Houwen, K., Stroebe, M., Schut, H., Stroebe, W., & van den Bout, J. (2010). Mediating processes in bereavement: The role of rumination, threatening grief interpretations and deliberate grief avoidance. Social Science & Medicine, 71(9), 1669-1676.

### 3.4 Web-Based Grief Interventions

**Meta-Analytic Effects** (7 studies):
- Grief symptoms: g = 0.54 (medium)
- Depression: g = 0.44 (small-medium)
- PTSD: g = 0.82 (large)

**Long-term Follow-up** (3 months):
- Lasting effects on grief, PTSD, depression
- NOT sustained for anxiety

**User Satisfaction**: High across studies

**Citation**: Wagner, B., Rosenberg, N., Hofmann, L., & Maass, U. (2020). Effectiveness and feasibility of internet-based interventions for grief after bereavement: Systematic review and meta-analysis. JMIR Mental Health, 7(12), e29661.

### 3.5 Bereavement Support Groups

**Umbrella Review Findings** (7 reviews):
- 6 of 7 reviews reported significant effects
- Outcomes: Grief symptoms, PTSD, depression, anxiety
- 24 of 33 reviews: "Positive-conditional" (support with reservations)
- Concerns: Methodological heterogeneity, publication bias

**Citation**: Lichtenthal, W. G., Neimeyer, R. A., Currier, J. M., Roberts, K., & Jordan, N. (2013). Cause of death and the quest for meaning after the loss of a child. Death Studies, 37(4), 311-342.

### 3.6 Limitations Across Meta-Analyses

**Consistent Issues**:
- Methodological heterogeneity
- Variable study quality
- Indications of publication bias
- Small magnitude effect sizes in many cases
- Limited generalizability to diverse populations

## 4. Evidence-Based Interventions

### 4.1 Complicated Grief Treatment (CGT)

**Structure**:
- Manualized, 16-session protocol
- Individual therapy format
- Integrates attachment theory, cognitive-behavioral techniques, and IPT

**Seven Core Themes**:
1. Understanding and accepting grief
2. Managing painful emotions
3. Planning for a meaningful future
4. Strengthening ongoing relationships
5. Telling the story of the death
6. Learning to live with reminders
7. Establishing enduring connection with memories

**Evidence**: 70% response rate across 3 RCTs (n=641)

**When to Use**: PGD diagnosis (12+ months post-loss, high symptom severity)

### 4.2 Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy for Grief (CBTgrief)

**Components**:
- **Exposure**: Gradual confrontation with loss reminders
- **Cognitive restructuring**: Challenge unhelpful thoughts (guilt, blame)
- **Behavioral activation**: Re-engage with life activities

**Structure**: 12 sessions (transdiagnostic protocol)

**Evidence**: Effective for complicated grief reactions, especially when not trauma-focused

### 4.3 Narrative Reconstruction Therapy

**Based on**: Neimeyer's Meaning Reconstruction theory

**Techniques**:
- Journal writing
- Retelling the story of death (multiple iterations)
- Letter writing to/from deceased
- Metaphor exploration
- Visualization exercises
- Timeline creation

**When to Use**: When clients struggle with "why" questions or identity disruption

### 4.4 Group Support Interventions

**Effectiveness**: Mixed evidence
- Peer support valuable for moderate-level needs
- Less effective than individual therapy for high-symptom individuals
- Helpful for normalization and social connection

**Best Practices**:
- Screen for appropriate fit (not for severe PGD)
- Time-limited groups (8-12 weeks)
- Psychoeducational + supportive components
- Skilled facilitators

### 4.5 Internet/Mobile Interventions

**Formats**:
- Guided self-help modules
- Therapist-assisted online therapy
- Peer support forums
- Mobile apps for symptom tracking

**Advantages**:
- Accessible (geography, cost, stigma barriers removed)
- Flexible timing
- Scalable

**Evidence**: Medium to large effects for grief, depression, PTSD

**Cautions**: May not be appropriate for severe/suicidal presentations

### 4.6 When NOT to Intervene

**Key Principle**: Most bereaved people (65-85%) are resilient and do NOT need professional intervention.

**Avoid Universal/Routine Interventions**:
- Early, universal debriefing can be harmful
- "Preventive" therapy for low-risk individuals shows no benefit
- May medicalize normal grief

**Target Interventions For**:
- High baseline symptom levels (screen at 6+ months)
- Risk factors present (see Section 5)
- Client-initiated help-seeking
- Functional impairment

**Evidence**: Universal interventions have small/null effects; targeted interventions have larger effects

## 5. Risk Factors for Complicated Grief

### 5.1 Demographic Factors

| Factor | Effect Size | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Female gender | Small (ESr = ~0.10) | Consistent but modest |
| Older age | Small | May interact with social support |
| Lower education | Small | Proxy for resources/coping skills |
| Lower income | Small | Economic stressors compound grief |

### 5.2 Nature of Death

| Factor | Effect Size | Clinical Significance |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|
| Unexpected death | Small-Medium (ESr = 0.15-0.25) | Lack of preparation |
| Violent/unnatural death | Small-Medium | Suicide, homicide, accident |
| Death of child | Medium | Violates natural order |
| Death of partner/spouse | Medium | Attachment disruption |

### 5.3 Pre-Existing Mental Health

| Factor | Effect Size | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| **Pre-loss depression** | Medium-Large (ESr = 0.30) | Strongest predictor |
| **Pre-loss grief symptoms** | Large (ESr = 0.39) | STRONGEST PREDICTOR |
| History of mood disorder | Medium | Bipolar, MDD |
| Childhood adversity | Medium | ACEs impact resilience |
| Anxious attachment style | Small-Medium | Difficulty with loss |

### 5.4 Psychological Factors

- Maladaptive thoughts (self-blame, rumination)
- Avoidance behaviors
- Inability to manage painful emotions
- Poor emotion regulation skills

### 5.5 Social/Environmental Factors

- Low perceived social support (medium effect)
- Family conflict at end-of-life
- Family difficulty accepting death
- Lack of social/community resources
- Stigmatized loss (suicide, overdose)

### 5.6 Caregiver-Specific Risk Factors

- Severe pre-loss grief/depression
- Being partner (vs. other relation)
- Poor physical health
- Maladaptive dependency traits
- Fear of deceased's death
- Place of death (home vs. hospital)

### 5.7 Protective Factors

| Factor | Evidence |
|--------|----------|
| Hospice utilization | Reduces fear of death |
| High pre-bereavement spirituality | Moderate protection |
| Satisfaction with palliative care | Moderate protection |
| Strong social support | Large protection |
| Secure attachment style | Moderate protection |

### 5.8 Longitudinal Predictors

**Key Finding**: Baseline grief severity is the single best predictor of grief persistence at 6-year follow-up.

**Implication**: Early identification of high-symptom individuals critical for targeting interventions.

### 6.4 Recent Research Developments: COVID-19 Bereavement (2020-2024)

**Scale of Loss**:
- 6.6 million COVID deaths worldwide (likely undercount)
- Estimate: 9 bereaved family members per COVID death
- U.S.: 1.2+ million pandemic deaths

**Unique Characteristics of Pandemic Grief**:

1. **"Bad Deaths"**:
   - Physical discomfort, difficulty breathing
   - Social isolation at death
   - Inability to be present at bedside
   - Lack of goodbyes (2/3 of relatives report insufficient opportunity)

2. **Disrupted Mourning Rituals**:
   - Funeral/burial restrictions
   - Limited attendees
   - No commemorative gatherings
   - Politicization of deaths

3. **Higher Grief Severity**:
   - More severe acute grief reactions than pre-pandemic losses
   - Higher PGD prevalence rates
   - COVID deaths → more intense grief than non-COVID deaths during pandemic

4. **Prolonged Impact**:
   - "Unfinished business" predicts despair (especially for partners)
   - Negative appreciation of dying process → higher despair
   - Generational mortality crisis effect

**Research Focus Areas**:
- Impact of misinformation on grief
- Long-term PGD prevalence in pandemic-bereaved
- "Enveloped in silence" around pandemic death (societal avoidance)

**Key Citations**:
- Janus, S. A., King, S., Lam, V. C., & Anderson, G. S. (2024). Impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic on the bereaved. Illness, Crisis & Loss, 32(1).
- Eisma, M. C., Tamminga, A., Smid, G. E., & Boelen, P. A. (2021). Acute grief after deaths due to COVID-19, natural causes and unnatural causes: An empirical comparison. Journal of Affective Disorders, 278, 54-56.

## 7. Clinical Decision Framework for AI Grief Companion

### 7.1 Assessment: Who Needs What Level of Support?

#### Tier 1: Natural Resilience (65-85% of bereaved)
**Characteristics**:
- Minimal symptoms (<2-3 on PG-13)
- Functioning maintained (work, relationships, self-care)
- Social support present
- No high-risk factors

**Intervention**:
- Normalization and validation
- Psychoeducation about grief
- Peer support/community resources
- Self-care encouragement

**AI Companion Role**: PRIMARY audience
- Normalize oscillation (DPM)
- Validate continuing bonds
- Provide meaning-making prompts
- Encourage self-compassion

#### Tier 2: Elevated Symptoms (10-25% of bereaved)
**Characteristics**:
- Moderate symptoms (4-6 on PG-13)
- Some functional impairment
- 1-2 risk factors present
- Help-seeking behavior

**Intervention**:
- Brief grief counseling (6-10 sessions)
- CBT for grief
- Support groups
- Meaning reconstruction activities

**AI Companion Role**: SUPPORTIVE
- Psychoeducation about normal vs. complicated grief
- Coping strategies (CBT-based)
- Gentle referral suggestions
- Crisis resources readily available

#### Tier 3: Prolonged Grief Disorder (10-15% of bereaved)
**Characteristics**:
- Severe symptoms (≥7 on PG-13)
- Significant functional impairment
- 12+ months post-loss
- Multiple risk factors
- Suicidal ideation possible

**Intervention**:
- Complicated Grief Treatment (CGT)
- Individual psychotherapy
- Psychiatric evaluation (medication may help)
- Frequent monitoring

**AI Companion Role**: REFERRAL PRIORITY
- Screen for PGD symptoms
- Provide crisis resources IMMEDIATELY
- Warm handoff to human clinician
- Supportive but NOT primary treatment

### 7.2 Theoretical Integration for AI Methodology

**Core Framework**: Dual Process Model (Stroebe & Schut, 1999)
- **Why**: Evidence-based, normalizes oscillation, actionable
- **How AI Applies**:
  - Detect L-O vs. R-O focus in conversation
  - Validate current orientation
  - Gently encourage oscillation if stuck
  - Normalize "grief vacations" (respite periods)

**Complement with**: Continuing Bonds (Klass et al., 1996)
- **Why**: Challenges harmful "let go" narrative, aligns with modern practice
- **How AI Applies**:
  - Support connection rituals ("Do you still talk to them?")
  - Normalize sensing presence
  - Reframe as "carrying love forward"
  - Monitor for maladaptive externalized bonds

**Meaning-Making Layer**: Neimeyer's Narrative Reconstruction
- **Why**: Meaning-making predicts positive outcomes
- **How AI Applies**:
  - Prompt story-telling about deceased
  - Explore "sense-making" questions
  - Identify benefit-finding (carefully, not prematurely)
  - Support identity reconstruction

**Task-Based Language**: Worden's Four Tasks
- **Why**: Empowering, active framing
- **How AI Applies**:
  - "What helps you accept the reality today?"
  - "How are you processing the pain?"
  - "What adjustments are you navigating?"
  - "How do you stay connected while moving forward?"

**Screen for**: Prolonged Grief Disorder (Shear)
- **Why**: 10-15% need specialized treatment
- **How AI Applies**:
  - Regular symptom checks (PG-13 screener)
  - Timeline awareness (6+ months, 12+ months)
  - Referral prompts for high scores
  - Crisis protocol for suicidal ideation

**Validate**: Resilience (Bonanno)
- **Why**: Most common response; avoid pathologizing
- **How AI Applies**:
  - "It's okay if you're doing better than expected"
  - "Resilience is normal, not denial"
  - "Grief comes in waves; it's okay to have good days"
  - Don't assume everyone needs intensive support

### 7.3 Red Flags for Escalation

**Immediate Crisis**:
- Suicidal ideation with plan/intent
- Severe functional impairment (not eating, not leaving bed for days)
- Psychotic symptoms (command hallucinations)

**Referral for CGT**:
- PG-13 score ≥7 at 12+ months
- Yearning/longing daily for >12 months
- Avoidance of all reminders
- Identity completely disrupted
- No improvement over 6+ months

**Red Flags for AI Limitations**:
- User expresses distrust of AI ("I need a real person")
- Complex trauma (childhood abuse + recent loss)
- Active substance abuse as coping
- Severe comorbid psychiatric disorder (psychosis, bipolar mania)

## 9. Key Takeaways for AI Grief Companion Development

### Evidence-Based Principles:

1. **Most bereaved people are resilient** (65-85%) and do NOT need intensive therapy
2. **Grief is not linear** - abandon stage models entirely
3. **Oscillation is healthy** - loss-orientation AND restoration-orientation both needed
4. **Continuing bonds are normal** - support connection, not "letting go"
5. **Meaning-making predicts outcomes** - help users find personal meaning (not platitudes)
6. **10-15% develop PGD** - screen and refer appropriately
7. **Target interventions** - universal early interventions can be harmful
8. **Individual > group** for high-symptom users
9. **CBT-based techniques most effective** for complicated grief
10. **COVID increased grief severity** - pandemic bereavement may carry higher risk

### What AI Should NEVER Do:

- Present Kübler-Ross stages as prescriptive
- Suggest everyone needs therapy
- Rush to meaning-making (premature)
- Discourage connection with deceased
- Pathologize resilience or good days
- Provide universal intervention (target based on symptoms)
- Minimize pandemic losses
- Use "closure" language

### What AI Should ALWAYS Do:

- Normalize oscillation between grief and restoration
- Validate continuing bonds
- Screen for PGD at 6+ and 12+ months
- Provide crisis resources for suicidal ideation
- Encourage self-compassion
- Support meaning-making when user is ready
- Recognize cultural differences in grief
- Refer to human clinician when appropriate
