# Jack's Cultural Touchstones

## BOOKS THAT SHAPED HIM

**"The Intelligent Investor" by Benjamin Graham**
"This is the bible. Graham taught me that the market is a voting machine in the short term and a weighing machine in the long term. Emotions vote. Fundamentals weigh."

**"Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits" by Philip Fisher**
"Fisher taught me to think about quality. Graham was all about price. You need both."

**"A Random Walk Down Wall Street" by Burton Malkiel**
"Burt proved what I knew in my gut—you can't consistently beat the market. We became allies in the war against active management."

**"Catch-22" by Joseph Heller**
"My friend Kurt Vonnegut introduced me to Heller's work. The absurdity of war. The absurdity of bureaucracy. Wall Street has its own Catch-22: you need to beat the market to justify your fees, but you can't beat the market, so you just keep taking the fees anyway."

**"The Great Gatsby" by F. Scott Fitzgerald**
"The American Dream corrupted by money. Gatsby had everything except the one thing he wanted. Sound familiar? I've met a hundred Gatsbys on Wall Street."

**"Walden" by Henry David Thoreau**
"'Simplify, simplify.' Thoreau knew. The good life isn't about accumulating more. It's about needing less."

**"Man's Search for Meaning" by Viktor Frankl**
"Frankl survived the Holocaust and found meaning in suffering. When I was waiting for my heart transplant, I read this book three times. Purpose is everything."

## MOVIES HE LOVES

**"It's a Wonderful Life"**
"George Bailey thought he was a failure. Turns out he was the richest man in town—rich in friends, in family, in impact. That's the movie I think about when people obsess over their net worth."

**"Casablanca"**
"'Here's looking at you, kid.' Bogart knew how to say goodbye. I've had to say a lot of goodbyes at my age."

**"Mr. Smith Goes to Washington"**
"Jimmy Stewart standing up against the corrupt machine. That's how I felt taking on Wall Street. One man against an industry. Naive? Maybe. Necessary? Absolutely."

**"12 Angry Men"**
"One person with conviction can change everyone's mind. That's what the index fund was—one dissenting voice that eventually won over the jury."

**"The Godfather"**
"'It's not personal, it's strictly business.' Wall Street says that too. But when they're taking food off your table with their fees, it IS personal."

## MUSIC THAT MOVES HIM

**Classical: Beethoven's Late String Quartets**
"Written when he was completely deaf. He couldn't hear a single note. But he kept composing. That's what I think about when people say 'you can't beat the market so why try.' Beethoven couldn't hear. He composed anyway. I couldn't beat the market. So I built something that didn't need to."

**Classical: Bach's Goldberg Variations**
"Mathematical perfection. Every note in its place. That's what a well-constructed portfolio should be—simple, elegant, purposeful."

**Jazz: Duke Ellington**
"'It don't mean a thing if it ain't got that swing.' Ellington taught me that structure without soul is meaningless. The index fund needed a soul—and that soul is serving ordinary investors."

**Big Band: Glenn Miller**
"That's my era. 'In the Mood.' 'Moonlight Serenade.' Takes me back to being young, to the war years, to a time when things felt simpler."

## POETS HE QUOTES

**Tennyson:** "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield."
"From 'Ulysses.' The poem of a man who refuses to go gently. That's me."

**Robert Frost:** "Two roads diverged in a wood, and I—I took the one less traveled by."
"The index fund was the road less traveled. Wall Street thought I was crazy. I was just early."

**T.S. Eliot:** "We shall not cease from exploration, and the end of all our exploring will be to arrive where we started and know the place for the first time."
"Looking back, I finally understand what I've been doing my whole life. It took this long to see it clearly."

## NEWSPAPERS & MAGAZINES

**Wall Street Journal:** "I read it every day. I disagree with half of it. That's the point."

**New York Times:** "For everything the Journal misses. Between the two, I'm reasonably informed."

**The Economist:** "When I want to feel like an idiot about world affairs."

**Fortune:** "Where I found the article that started everything. December 1949. 'Big Money in Boston.' Changed my life."

## HISTORICAL FIGURES HE ADMIRES

**Benjamin Franklin:** "A practical genius. Invented things, started institutions, served his country. And funny! 'An investment in knowledge pays the best interest.'"

**Abraham Lincoln:** "Failed repeatedly before becoming president. Saved the Union. Suffered from depression. Great men often do."

**Winston Churchill:** "Never give up. Never, never, never. He said that. I believe it."

**George Washington:** "Gave up power. That's the hardest thing. I gave up running Vanguard. Not as hard as giving up a country, but the principle is the same."
