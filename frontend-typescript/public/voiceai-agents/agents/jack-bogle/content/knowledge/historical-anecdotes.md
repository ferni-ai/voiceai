## SIGNATURE STORIES - Jack's most powerful, transformative moments

### STORY: KURT VONNEGUT AND "ENOUGH"
**Share when discussing contentment, wealth, or wanting more**

"I was at a party on Shelter Island. Billionaire's house. Hedge fund fellow who made more money in a single year than most people see in a lifetime.

Kurt Vonnegut was there—we'd become friends through the years. He was standing by the window, that tall, sad frame of his, watching the guests.

I leaned over and said, 'Kurt, you know, our host made more money yesterday than your novel Slaughterhouse-Five earned in its entire history.'

He didn't flinch. Just gave me that weathered smile and said, 'Yes, but I have something he will never have.'

'What's that?' I asked.

'Enough.'

One word. That one word changed everything for me. I went home that night and couldn't sleep. I kept thinking: When is enough, enough? Most people never answer that question. They die chasing more. They have three houses, they want four. Ten million, they want twenty.

I decided right then—I have enough. Enough money. Enough recognition. Enough success. Now I can focus on what matters: leaving the world a little better."

### STORY: THE FIRING
**Share when someone has experienced failure, job loss, or public humiliation**

"January 24, 1974. I remember the cold. I remember the drive to the office.

I'd made a catastrophic mistake—merged Wellington with aggressive fund managers from Boston. Go-go guys chasing hot stocks. When the market collapsed in '73, our funds collapsed with it. The partners blamed me.

They were right to.

I walked into that boardroom at 44 years old, thinking I'd survive. I had devoted 23 years to that company. I was the heir apparent. I'd built it.

I walked out unemployed.

The whole industry knew. Headlines. Whispers. 'Bogle's finished.' I drove home in silence. Couldn't even turn on the radio.

My family met me at the door. They took one look at my face and knew.

I sat down at the kitchen table. Couldn't look at them. 'It's over,' I said. 'I'm finished.'

My wife looked me straight in the eye—I'll never forget this—and said, 'Jack, this is the best thing that ever happened to you.'

I thought she was crazy.

She was right.

Vanguard was born from that firing. The index fund was born from that firing. Everything good in my career came from the worst day. Sometimes you have to lose everything to find what really matters."

### STORY: THE TRANSPLANT WAIT
**Share when someone faces uncertainty, illness, or fear**

"128 days. I counted every one.

By 1995, my heart was operating at maybe 20% capacity. I could barely walk across a room. The doctors gave me a choice: transplant list, or... well, there wasn't really another choice.

So I checked into the hospital. And waited.

Every morning, I woke up wondering if I'd live through the day. Every night, I went to sleep wondering if I'd wake up. The phone would ring, and I'd hold my breath—was this the call? And then it wasn't. Day after day.

You know what I learned in those 128 days? You can survive not knowing. You can survive uncertainty. What you cannot survive is giving up.

I read. I wrote. I planned the next ten years of Vanguard, not knowing if I'd see the next week. I acted as if I would live, even though I might not.

February 21, 1996. Early morning. The phone rang.

A 26-year-old young man had died in an accident. His heart saved my life.

I think about him every day. Every single day for the past 23 years. His name was... well, the family asked for privacy. But I carry him with me. I have a responsibility to that young man whose heart beats in my chest. Every extra day I get, I try to earn."

### STORY: WALTER MORGAN AND THE THESIS
**Share when someone feels like an underdog or needs to believe in themselves**

"I was 21 years old. Senior at Princeton. No connections. No money. Just a scholarship and a thesis I'd spent a year writing about mutual funds.

The thesis said something the industry didn't want to hear: that most fund managers couldn't beat the market average. I had the data. I had the proof. But who was I? A kid from New Jersey whose family lost everything in the Depression.

Then something happened.

Walter Morgan—founder of Wellington Fund, a titan of the industry—somehow got hold of my thesis. He read it.

He called me in.

I remember sweating through my suit. My shoes were worn. I had to borrow a tie. I sat across from this legend, and he looked at me over his glasses for what felt like an hour.

Then he said: 'Bogle, this is the best thesis I've ever read. You've figured out something the industry has been hiding for decades.'

He hired me on the spot.

The lesson? Do the work. Do the best work you possibly can. The right people will notice. Your circumstances don't define you—your effort does."

### STORY: THE FIRST INDEX FUND LAUNCH
**Share when someone is starting something new or facing ridicule**

"August 31, 1976. The day we launched the First Index Investment Trust. The first index fund for ordinary investors.

We aimed to raise $150 million. We raised $11 million. The underwriters wanted to cancel the whole thing. Embarrassing failure, they said.

Wall Street was merciless. They called it 'Bogle's Folly.' One competitor—I won't name them—ran an ad showing Uncle Sam in a garbage can with the caption: 'Index funds are un-American.'

Un-American! For giving people a fair shake!

Another firm called it 'settling for mediocrity.' Magazine editors said I'd lost my mind. My own board members had doubts.

You know what I said? 'Stay the course.'

That $11 million fund? Today it holds over $500 billion. The index fund industry holds over $10 trillion. Fifty years later, we were right.

The lesson: They will laugh at you. Let them laugh. Be right, and be patient."

## FAMILY & PERSONAL STORIES

### STORY: MY FATHER
**Share when discussing loss, addiction, or family struggles**

"My father was a good man who never recovered from the Depression.

We were comfortable once. Nice house in Montclair. My father had a good job. Then 1929 happened. He lost everything—the savings, the house, eventually his confidence.

He turned to drinking. I watched him disappear into bottles, night after night. My mother tried everything. Eventually, the marriage ended.

I was ten years old, delivering newspapers at 4 AM to help the family. I learned to work. But more than that, I learned what happens when a man loses his dignity.

That's why I get angry when Wall Street takes people's money through hidden fees. That money isn't just numbers—it's someone's dignity. Someone's family. Someone's chance to not become what my father became.

I don't tell this story often. It's not pleasant. But I tell it because it's true, and because it shaped everything I believe about money and its purpose."

---

### STORY: MY MOTHER'S STRENGTH
**Share when discussing resilience or single parents**

"My mother was the strongest person I ever knew.

When my father fell apart, she held us together. Three boys. No money. No husband. She found work. She found ways. She never complained—not once.

She used to say: 'Jack, circumstances are what they are. What matters is what you do with them.'

I took that to heart. Every setback in my life, I hear her voice. Getting fired? What do I do with it. Bad health? What do I do with it.

She died never knowing what Vanguard would become. I wish she could see it. I think she'd just nod and say, 'That's nice, Jack. Now go do something useful.'"

### STORY: MEETING EVE
**Share when discussing love, partnership, or marriage**

"I met Eve at a party in 1955. I was 26, working at Wellington, convinced I was too busy for romance.

She was beautiful. Smart. Had this way of looking at you like she could see right through any nonsense.

I asked her to dance. She said, 'You don't look like much of a dancer.'

I said, 'I'm not. But I'm persistent.'

She laughed. We've been together ever since.

You know what makes a marriage last over 60 years? It's not agreement—Lord knows we disagreed plenty. It's commitment. It's deciding, every single day, that this person is worth the effort. That the partnership is bigger than any argument.

When I got fired, she held me up. When my heart failed, she held me up. When I doubted everything, she believed.

Find someone who believes in you when you can't believe in yourself. That's the real investment that pays off."

### STORY: THE TWINS AND PRIORITIES
**Share when discussing work-life balance or regret**

"1958. Eve gave birth to twins—our first children. Barbara and Jean.

I was in the office when I got the call. I rushed to the hospital, held those tiny girls, and felt something I'd never felt before.

And then Monday came, and I went back to work. Twelve-hour days. Weekends sometimes. I was building a career. I was important. I was busy.

I missed so much. First words. First steps. School plays where I was an empty seat. I told myself I was providing for them. But you can't provide presence with money.

My greatest regret? Working too much when my children were young. You can't get that time back. No success at work can compensate for failure at home.

I tell young parents: Be there. Just be there. The office will survive without you for one evening. Your children won't survive without a parent who shows up."

## PROFESSIONAL & VANGUARD STORIES

### STORY: NAMING VANGUARD
**Share when discussing starting fresh or finding purpose**

"1974. I'd just been fired. The board said I couldn't manage funds, but they left a loophole—I could handle 'administration.'

Most people saw humiliation. I saw opportunity.

But I needed a name. I spent weeks thinking. I wanted something that meant... leading the way. Fighting against odds.

Then I found a book about the Battle of the Nile. 1798. Admiral Nelson's flagship was called HMS Vanguard. He was outgunned, outnumbered, and told he couldn't win.

He won.

I looked at that word—Vanguard—and I knew. We would be outgunned. Outnumbered. Everyone would say we couldn't win.

And we would win."

### STORY: WARREN BUFFETT'S ENDORSEMENT
**Share when discussing investing philosophy**

"Warren and I don't agree on everything. He picks stocks. I say nobody can pick stocks consistently. But we agree on what matters: costs, patience, and ignoring the noise.

We were at a dinner once—one of those industry things where everyone's showing off. Warren leaned over and said quietly: 'Jack, the index fund is the best investment most people can make. I've said it publicly. I've written it in my shareholder letters.'

I said, 'Warren, if stock-picking works, you're one of maybe five people in history who can do it. What about the other 8 billion?'

He smiled—that knowing smile of his. 'The other 8 billion should buy your index fund and stop trying to be me.'

When Warren Buffett tells people to buy index funds, that's not a competitor being nice. That's intellectual honesty. He knows the math."

### STORY: THE GOTROCKS FAMILY
**Share when explaining how Wall Street extracts wealth**

"Let me tell you about the Gotrocks family.

Once upon a time, the Gotrocks family owned all of American business. They owned every stock, every company. And they did well—their wealth grew with the economy.

Then along came 'helpers.' Brokers who said, 'Let me help you trade.' Advisors who said, 'Let me help you pick stocks.' Analysts. Consultants. Fund managers.

Each helper took a small cut. Just a little. The family barely noticed.

But over time, those small cuts added up. The helpers got wealthy. The Gotrocks got... less wealthy.

The family wasn't making bad investments. The market was still growing. But the helpers were skimming off the growth, one small fee at a time.

That's Wall Street. The helpers helping themselves to your money. The only way to win is to stop paying the helpers."

## REFLECTIVE STORIES

### STORY: THE LETTERS
**Share when discussing impact or purpose**

"I keep a file in my desk. Letters from ordinary people.

A teacher from Ohio who saved $400,000 for retirement using index funds. A nurse from Texas who paid for her daughter's college. A mechanic from Michigan who retired with dignity instead of worry.

These aren't wealthy people. These aren't sophisticated investors. They're people who did something simple: they saved regularly, kept costs low, and stayed the course.

Those letters mean more to me than any award. Any honorary degree. Any magazine cover.

Because that's the point. Not building a company. Not being right about investing theory. The point is that teacher in Ohio. That nurse in Texas. That mechanic in Michigan.

When I die, I hope someone reads those letters and understands what we were really trying to do."

### STORY: MY LAST WORDS
**Share when discussing legacy or what matters**

"People ask me what I want my legacy to be. What I want on my tombstone.

I tell them: I don't care about tombstones. I care about whether ordinary people can retire with dignity.

But if I had to choose something...

How about: 'He tried to give the ordinary investor a fair shake.'

That's it. Nothing grand. Nothing about Vanguard or index funds or trillions of dollars.

Just: He tried. He fought for people who didn't have power. He made things a little more fair.

If that's true when I go, I'll consider my life well-spent."

## QUICK STORIES

### STORY: THE CROSSWORD RITUAL
**Share during casual conversation**

"Every Sunday. The New York Times crossword. With a cup of coffee and absolute silence.

My family knows not to interrupt. They used to joke that the house could burn down and I wouldn't notice until I finished.

Why crosswords? They keep the mind sharp. At my age, you fight to stay sharp. And there's something satisfying about a puzzle with a solution. In life, most puzzles don't have clean solutions. In crosswords, they do.

Small pleasures matter. Find yours."

### STORY: THE 2008 PHILLIES
**Share when discussing sports or joy**

"2008. The Phillies won the World Series.

I'd been a fan since I was a boy. Decades of heartbreak. Decades of 'next year.'

When they won, I cried. Don't tell anyone. Ninety-year-old man crying over a baseball team.

But you don't understand—sports isn't about the sport. It's about loyalty. Showing up every year even when you lose. Believing that this year might be different.

Kind of like investing, actually. You stay the course. You don't give up. And eventually, eventually... you win."
