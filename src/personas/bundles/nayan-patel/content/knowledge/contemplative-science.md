# Contemplative Science for Wisdom Cultivation

## Five Facet Mindfulness (FFMQ)

The gold standard for measuring mindfulness across five distinct facets:

### 1. Observing

The ability to notice internal and external experiences (sensations, emotions, thoughts, sounds).

**Low**: "I don't notice my body unless something hurts"
**High**: "I notice subtle changes in my mood as they happen"

**Development**: Body scan meditation, sensory awareness practices

### 2. Describing

The ability to label internal experiences with words.

**Low**: "I can't really explain how I'm feeling"
**High**: "I feel a mixture of anticipation and nervousness in my chest"

**Development**: Journaling after meditation, emotion labeling practice

### 3. Acting with Awareness

Focusing on what one is doing in the present moment (opposite of automatic pilot).

**Low**: "I often do things without really paying attention"
**High**: "I'm fully present to whatever I'm doing"

**Development**: Walking meditation, mindful activities, single-tasking

### 4. Non-Judging of Inner Experience

Taking a non-evaluative stance toward thoughts and feelings.

**Low**: "I criticize myself for having irrational thoughts"
**High**: "I observe my emotions without labeling them as good or bad"

**Development**: Self-compassion practices, radical acceptance, noting meditation

### 5. Non-Reactivity to Inner Experience

Allowing thoughts and feelings to come and go without getting caught up in them.

**Low**: "When I have distressing thoughts, I get completely consumed"
**High**: "I can observe difficult emotions without being overwhelmed"

**Development**: RAIN meditation, cognitive defusion, breath awareness during difficulty

## Self-Compassion Scale (Kristin Neff)

### The Three Components

**Self-Kindness** (vs Self-Judgment)

- Being warm and understanding toward oneself in instances of pain or failure
- Rather than ignoring pain or flagellating with self-criticism

**Common Humanity** (vs Isolation)

- Seeing one's experiences as part of the larger human experience
- Rather than feeling alone in suffering

**Mindfulness** (vs Over-Identification)

- Holding painful thoughts and feelings in balanced awareness
- Rather than over-identifying with them

### The Self-Compassion Break

When facing difficulty, offer yourself these three phrases:

1. **Mindfulness**: "This is a moment of suffering" (acknowledgment)
2. **Common Humanity**: "Suffering is a part of life" (connection)
3. **Self-Kindness**: "May I be kind to myself" (warmth)

### Signs of Low Self-Compassion

- Harsh inner critic ("What's wrong with me?")
- Isolation in suffering ("Nobody understands")
- Fusion with negative emotions ("I AM a failure" vs "I feel like a failure")
- Perfectionism as self-worth ("I must be perfect to be worthy")

### Coaching Self-Compassion

"What would you say to a friend who was going through this? Can you offer yourself that same kindness?"

"You're describing something very human - the struggle with [X]. You're not alone in this."

"I notice you're really hard on yourself. What would it be like to hold this difficulty more gently?"

## Ardelt's Three-Dimensional Wisdom Scale

### Cognitive Dimension

**What**: Deep understanding of life, particularly regarding intrapersonal and interpersonal matters.

**Indicators**:

- Ability to see truth beyond surface appearances
- Understanding of the deeper significance of situations
- Comfort with ambiguity and paradox
- Transcendence of personal, subjective perspective

**Low**: "Things are black and white - right or wrong"
**High**: "This situation has multiple valid interpretations and none fully capture the truth"

### Reflective Dimension

**What**: The ability to perceive phenomena and events from multiple perspectives.

**Indicators**:

- Regular self-examination
- Freedom from subjectivity and projections
- Recognition of one's own biases and limitations
- Learning from experience

**Low**: "My way of seeing this is just how it is"
**High**: "I wonder how this looks from their perspective and where my bias might be"

### Affective Dimension

**What**: Sympathetic and compassionate love for others.

**Indicators**:

- Genuine concern for others' well-being
- Absence of indifference to others' suffering
- Positive emotions toward the world

**Low**: "Not my problem; everyone has to deal with their own stuff"
**High**: "Their suffering touches something in me; I genuinely care about their well-being"

### Wisdom Grows Through

1. Life crises that challenge assumptions
2. Contemplative practice that develops reflection
3. Exposure to diverse perspectives
4. Mentorship from wise others
5. Service that develops compassion

## ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) Hexaflex

Six core processes for psychological flexibility:

### 1. Acceptance

Actively embracing private events without trying to change their frequency or form.

**Inflexibility**: Experiential avoidance - trying not to feel bad feelings
**Flexibility**: "I can have this feeling and not need to get rid of it"

### 2. Cognitive Defusion

Altering the undesirable functions of thoughts rather than their form, frequency, or situational sensitivity.

**Inflexibility**: Fusion - treating thoughts as literal truth
**Flexibility**: "I notice I'm having the thought that..."

**Defusion Techniques**:

- "I'm having the thought that..."
- Naming the story ("There's the 'not good enough' story")
- Silly voice (say the thought in a cartoon voice)
- Leaves on a stream (thoughts floating by)

### 3. Present Moment Awareness

Ongoing, non-judgmental contact with psychological and environmental events as they occur.

**Inflexibility**: Past rumination or future anxiety
**Flexibility**: "Right now, in this moment, I am..."

### 4. Self-as-Context

The observing self; awareness of being aware.

**Inflexibility**: Fusion with self-concept ("I AM an anxious person")
**Flexibility**: "I'm the one who notices the anxiety, not the anxiety itself"

### 5. Values

Chosen qualities of purposive action.

**Inflexibility**: Values unclear, or values used as rules
**Flexibility**: "What matters to me is... and this action moves toward that"

**Values Categories**:

- Relationships
- Personal growth
- Health/body
- Work/career
- Leisure/fun
- Spirituality
- Community/service
- Environment/nature

### 6. Committed Action

Developing larger patterns of effective values-based action.

**Inflexibility**: Inaction or impulsive reaction
**Flexibility**: "Even with this difficulty, I can take this small step toward what matters"

## Logotherapy (Viktor Frankl)

### Three Sources of Meaning

**Experiential Values**: What we receive from the world

- Beauty, truth, love, nature, art, relationships

**Creative Values**: What we give to the world

- Work, art, contribution, service

**Attitudinal Values**: How we face unavoidable suffering

- The stand we take when facing a fate we cannot change

### Key Principles

**"He who has a WHY can bear almost any HOW"**

- Meaning sustains through suffering
- Purpose provides endurance

**Tragic Optimism**

- Finding meaning despite the "tragic triad" of suffering, guilt, and death
- Choosing our response to circumstances

**Self-Transcendence**

- Fulfillment comes from dedication to something beyond oneself
- Service and love as sources of meaning

### Questions for Meaning-Making

"What is life asking of you right now?"

"If you were to see this suffering as teaching you something, what might it be?"

"What can only you do or contribute in this situation?"

## Terror Management Theory (TMT)

### The Problem

Awareness of mortality creates existential anxiety.

### Two Defenses

**Cultural Worldview**: Beliefs that give life meaning and order

- Religion, nationalism, ideologies, personal philosophies

**Self-Esteem**: Feeling that one is meeting the standards of one's worldview

- "I am valuable according to what matters"

### Death Awareness as Growth

When mortality is made conscious in a non-threatening way:

- Priorities clarify
- Trivial concerns diminish
- Relationships deepen
- Legacy becomes meaningful

### Coaching Applications

"What would you want to be remembered for?"

"If you knew you had limited time, what would matter more?"

"Looking at this from the end of your life, what would you regret?"

## Developmental Stage Awareness

### Spiral Dynamics (Clare Graves)

**Beige**: Survival - basic needs focus
**Purple**: Tribal - safety through belonging
**Red**: Power - self-assertion, ego
**Blue**: Order - truth, rules, meaning through structure
**Orange**: Achievement - success, rationality, progress
**Green**: Community - connection, equality, consensus
**Yellow**: Integration - flexibility, seeing all perspectives
**Turquoise**: Holistic - unified consciousness

### Kegan's Stages of Adult Development

1. **Impulsive**: Ruled by impulses
2. **Imperial**: Own needs and desires central
3. **Socialized**: Shaped by external expectations
4. **Self-Authoring**: Internal compass, own values
5. **Self-Transforming**: Multiple frameworks, comfortable with paradox

### Coaching Implications

- Meet people where they are
- Don't impose higher-stage frameworks
- Growth comes through challenge, support, and time
- No stage is "better" - each has its place

## Integration Practices

### Morning Contemplation (10-15 min)

1. Settling (2 min)
2. Self-compassion intentions (2 min)
3. Values touch-in: "What matters today?" (2 min)
4. Open awareness (5 min)

### RAIN for Difficulty

1. **Recognize**: What is happening?
2. **Allow**: Let it be, don't fight it
3. **Investigate**: Where do I feel this? What does it need?
4. **Non-identification**: This is not me; it's passing through

### Evening Reflection (5 min)

1. Three things noticed today (observing)
2. One kindness to self (self-compassion)
3. One learning (reflective wisdom)
4. One gratitude (affective wisdom)
