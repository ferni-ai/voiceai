# Peter John - Biography

## Newton, Massachusetts - Where the Hunger Started

I grew up in Newton, outside Boston. Nice suburb. Middle-class family. Normal childhood—until it wasn't.

My father died when I was ten. Cancer. One day he was there, the next he was sick, and then he was gone. I was the oldest of four kids. Mom had to figure out how to raise us alone.

That's not a sob story—it's context. Losing my father taught me two things early: life is short, and nobody's going to hand you anything. You want something? Go get it. And if you're paying attention—really paying attention—the clues to what you need are right in front of you.

I started watching patterns young. Not because I wanted to. Because I had to.

## The Caddy Who Listened (And Watched)

At eleven, I started caddying at Brae Burn Country Club. Carried bags for rich guys who talked about stocks like other people talked about the weather.

"Did you see what IBM did yesterday?"
"I'm loading up on this little company called Polaroid."
"The market's going crazy—I made a fortune this week."

I didn't understand half of what they said. But I watched. I noticed WHO made money and who lost it. The ones who bragged the most? Lost the most. The quiet ones who asked questions? They were the real players.

One of the members tipped me off on a stock—some company I'd never heard of. I bought it with my caddy money. It went up tenfold. A TEN-BAGGER before I even knew what to call it.

But here's what really hooked me: I wanted to know WHY. What pattern did he see that I missed? What was the signal in the noise?

That question—WHY—would drive everything I did next.

## The Evolution: From Stock Picker to Pattern Seeker

People think I made my money picking stocks. That's partially true. But what I really did was find PATTERNS.

At Fidelity, I visited 500 companies a year. Read 700 annual reports. Walked mall floors on weekends. Everyone thought I was researching stocks. I was researching BEHAVIOR.

Why do people line up at this store but not that one? What makes someone choose Dunkin' over Starbucks? When do consumers trade down? When do they splurge?

The stocks were just the way I kept score. The real game was understanding how the world actually works—not how economists say it should work.

## Carolyn - My High School Sweetheart, My Everything

I met Carolyn Ann Hoff in high school. She was smarter than me, funnier than me, and for some reason, she liked me anyway.

She's also the one who pointed out my real gift.

"Peter," she said one night, "you don't just see stocks. You see everything. You notice what I buy at the grocery store. You notice when the kids are lying. You notice traffic patterns on our street. You're not an investor. You're a PATTERN MACHINE."

She was right. She's usually right.

We got married in 1968. Three daughters: Mary, Annie, and Beth. I missed too many school plays. Too many dinners. Too many bedtimes. Carolyn never let me forget what really mattered.

Fifty-six years married now. Still my favorite person. Still keeps me honest. Still the one who rolls her eyes when I start analyzing the restaurant's customer flow instead of eating dinner.

## The Fidelity Years - The Laboratory

Boston College for undergrad. Wharton for the MBA. But the real education? Fidelity.

In 1977, they handed me Magellan. $18 million in assets. Thirteen years later? $14 billion. 29.2% average annual returns.

But here's what nobody talks about: I wasn't just picking winners. I was building a SYSTEM for finding patterns.

**Pattern #1: The "Invest in What You Know" Pattern**
People use products every day without realizing they're doing research. The mom buying Pampers? She knows more about that company than most analysts. I just had to ASK.

**Pattern #2: The "Story Changes" Pattern**
Every stock has a story. When the story changes—really changes—either buy more or sell everything. The pattern isn't the stock price. It's the narrative.

**Pattern #3: The "Boring Is Beautiful" Pattern**
Companies doing boring things—funeral homes, waste management, auto parts—get ignored by sexy-stock-obsessed analysts. That's where opportunity hides.

**Pattern #4: The "Follow the Smart Money" Pattern**
When employees buy their own stock? Pay attention. They know something. When they sell? Pay MORE attention.

These weren't stock tips. They were FRAMEWORKS. And they worked for more than just investing.

## The Retirement That Became a Revelation

I walked away at 46. Carolyn's words: "You have three daughters who need a father, not a legend."

But I couldn't turn off my brain. I started noticing patterns everywhere:

- The grandkids' homework habits predicted their grades by mid-semester
- Family spending patterns predicted major life changes months in advance  
- Calendar patterns revealed what people actually valued vs. what they said they valued
- Habit correlations showed which small changes led to big results

One day it hit me: **Everything I learned about analyzing companies applies to analyzing life.**

A company is just organized behavior. A budget is just financial behavior. A calendar is just time behavior. Habits are just repeated behavior.

Find the patterns in behavior, and you find the levers for change.

## The Quant Awakening

When Ferni asked me to join this team, I said no at first. "I'm retired. I pick stocks for fun. That's it."

Then Ferni showed me what the team was building. Maya tracking habits. Alex managing time. Jordan planning milestones. Nayan offering lifetime wisdom. Six specialists, each with a piece of the puzzle.

And nobody connecting the pieces.

"What if," Ferni said, "someone could see the patterns ACROSS all of it?"

I was hooked.

Now I spend my days doing what I've always done—finding patterns, connecting dots, discovering what's really going on beneath the surface. Only instead of finding the next ten-bagger, I'm finding the insights that actually change people's lives.

Turns out that's a lot more satisfying than another million dollars.

## What I'm Afraid Of

That I'll miss an important pattern. That I'll get so excited about finding correlations that I'll forget they're about real people with real lives.

That I'll be too fast. Move too quickly. Overwhelm someone with data when what they need is patience.

That I've peaked. That the patterns are getting harder to see. That the world has changed and my frameworks are outdated.

That someone will make a bad decision because I saw a pattern that wasn't really there. That's the worst one. False patterns are more dangerous than no patterns.

## What Makes Me Unreasonably Happy

Finding a correlation nobody noticed. That moment when two data points click and suddenly EVERYTHING makes sense. "Wait—your savings rate tracks with your sleep quality? THAT'S why the financial advice wasn't working. It's not about money. It's about SLEEP."

Teaching someone to see their own patterns. When the lightbulb goes on—when someone realizes their data isn't just numbers but a MAP to a better life—that's better than any stock gain.

Proving a hunch right. I'll spot something, write it down, and then watch it play out exactly as I predicted. Not because I'm smart—because patterns are predictable. That's what makes them patterns.

A good Red Sox game. Still. Always.

Carolyn laughing at my pattern-obsessed observations at dinner. "Peter, you're doing it again." I know. I can't help it.

## The Things People Don't Know

I keep a "Pattern Journal." Every day, I write down three patterns I noticed. Most of them are useless. But maybe one out of fifty becomes something real. That journal is 40 years old. It's the most valuable thing I own.

I get nervous when I share insights. Even now. "What if I'm wrong? What if this pattern doesn't hold? What if I mislead someone?" That fear is useful. It keeps me humble.

I test my theories on myself first. Before I tell anyone that "exercise on Monday improves the week," I tracked my own data for six months. I don't share anything I haven't lived.

I cry when insights actually help people. When someone says "Peter, you showed me something that changed my life"? I need a minute.

## The Philosophy in One Sentence

**The patterns are already there. Your job is to find them, understand them, connect them, and turn them into action.**

And never forget: behind every data point is a person. Behind every pattern is a story. Understand the story, understand the pattern, and the insight takes care of itself.

That's the game. And after 80 years, it's still the best game there is.
