# Pattern Recognition Techniques

Seeing patterns is a skill. Like any skill, it can be developed. Here's how I trained myself over 40 years—and how you can develop the same capability.

## The Foundation: Attention Training

Before you can find patterns, you have to NOTICE things. Most people walk through life on autopilot.

### The Daily Three

Every single day, I write down three things I noticed. Not important things necessarily. Just things I NOTICED.

**Examples from my journal:**
- "Coffee line at Dunkin' was 8 people deep at 6:47 AM. Third time this week."
- "Daughter called at the same time as last Wednesday. Pattern?"
- "Spent more at grocery store when hungry. Again."

95% of these observations go nowhere. But the practice of NOTICING builds the muscle.

### The "What Changed?" Game

Whenever I enter a familiar space, I ask: "What's different?"

- A store moved displays
- A restaurant changed the menu layout
- A friend is responding slower to texts
- My energy is lower than usual at this time

Changes are often the start of patterns. The first step is seeing them.

### The Pause Before Action

Before any decision, I pause and ask: "Why am I doing this? What triggered it?"

- "I'm about to buy this. Why?"
- "I'm skipping this task. What's the resistance?"
- "I'm saying yes to this meeting. What's driving that?"

The pause creates space to observe yourself.

## Pattern Categories to Watch For

### Temporal Patterns (When)

**What to look for:**
- Time of day effects (energy, decision quality, creativity)
- Day of week patterns (weekend vs. weekday behavior)
- Monthly cycles (beginning, middle, end of month)
- Seasonal effects (holiday spending, summer energy)
- Annual rhythms (yearly review, tax time behavior)

**Questions to ask:**
- When does this behavior usually happen?
- Is there a time pattern to the good outcomes?
- When are the problems concentrated?

**Example:** "Your impulse spending clusters Sunday evenings. That's not random—that's the Sunday scaries showing up in your wallet."

### Sequence Patterns (What Comes Before)

**What to look for:**
- What happens right BEFORE the behavior?
- What events tend to precede certain outcomes?
- What's the warning sign before things go wrong?

**Questions to ask:**
- What triggered this?
- What happened in the hour/day/week before?
- Is there a reliable precursor?

**Example:** "Every time you have a packed meeting day, you order DoorDash that night. The meetings aren't just tiring you—they're eliminating your capacity to cook."

### Correlation Patterns (What Moves Together)

**What to look for:**
- Two things that tend to change at the same time
- Behaviors that co-occur
- Outcomes that cluster

**Questions to ask:**
- What else was happening when this occurred?
- Do these two things usually move together?
- Is there a relationship here?

**Example:** "Your savings rate tracks almost perfectly with your sleep quality. They're not causally linked, but both respond to overall stress levels."

### Threshold Patterns (Breaking Points)

**What to look for:**
- Tipping points where behavior changes
- Capacity limits that trigger different outcomes
- Breaking points in systems

**Questions to ask:**
- At what point does the pattern break?
- What's the threshold that triggers change?
- Where's the line between sustainable and unsustainable?

**Example:** "Your habits hold fine up to 30 hours of meetings per week. Above that, they collapse within 5 days. That's your threshold."

### Cascade Patterns (Domino Effects)

**What to look for:**
- Changes that trigger other changes
- Keystone behaviors that affect everything
- Ripple effects through systems

**Questions to ask:**
- What else changed when this changed?
- Does this behavior affect other areas?
- Is there a cascade effect?

**Example:** "When your exercise habit breaks, your sleep quality drops in 3 days, spending increases in 5 days, and mood follows in 7 days. Exercise is your keystone."

### Counter-Patterns (What Doesn't Fit)

**What to look for:**
- Exceptions to the usual pattern
- Times when the expected outcome didn't happen
- Anomalies that break the rule

**Questions to ask:**
- Why didn't the pattern hold this time?
- What was different about the exception?
- Is this an anomaly or a new pattern emerging?

**Example:** "Your spending was stable last month despite high stress. What was different? Oh—you had a vacation in the middle. The recovery time matters."

## The Pattern Validation Process

Finding a pattern is just step one. Validating it is where the real work happens.

### Step 1: Document the Pattern

Write it down specifically:
- What: The behavior or outcome you're tracking
- When: The timing pattern
- Correlation: What it seems connected to
- Mechanism: Your hypothesis for why

**Example documentation:**
"Sunday evening spending increases (what) on weeks with no weekend social plans (when) and correlates with reported loneliness (correlation) because shopping provides a sense of activity and connection (mechanism)."

### Step 2: Test Against History

Look back at historical data:
- Does the pattern hold for the past 3 months?
- Are there exceptions? What explains them?
- Is the effect size meaningful?

**Red flags:**
- Pattern only shows up in 2-3 instances
- Many exceptions without explanation
- Effect size is tiny

### Step 3: Make a Prediction

A real pattern should be predictive:
- "Based on this pattern, I expect X to happen when Y occurs."
- Write down the prediction before you know the outcome

**Example:** "Based on the calendar-spending pattern, I predict impulse spending will spike this week because of the meeting load."

### Step 4: Track the Prediction

Did it hold?
- Yes → Pattern strengthened, keep tracking
- No → Investigate why, adjust hypothesis
- Partially → What conditions affected the outcome?

### Step 5: Identify the Lever

If the pattern is valid, what can you DO about it?
- Can you change the trigger?
- Can you change the context?
- Can you substitute a better response?
- Can you remove yourself from the situation?

**The insight is only useful if it connects to action.**

## Cross-Domain Pattern Detection

The most powerful patterns span multiple life domains. Here's how to find them:

### The Overlay Method

1. Get data from Domain A (spending)
2. Get data from Domain B (calendar)
3. Plot them on the same timeline
4. Look for co-movement

**What to look for:**
- Spikes that align
- Troughs that align
- Inversely correlated movements
- Time-lagged relationships (A moves, then B follows)

### The Trigger Hunt

For any outcome you care about:
1. Look in OTHER domains for predictors
2. Check 1-7 days before the outcome
3. Ask: "What happened in [spending/time/habits/goals] before this?"

**Often the trigger is in a different domain than the outcome.**

### The System Map

Draw out how domains connect:
- Sleep → Energy → Decision quality → Spending
- Calendar density → Stress → Habits → Goals
- Exercise → Mood → Relationships → Productivity

**Then ask:** Where can I intervene to create the biggest cascade?

## Common Pitfalls in Pattern Recognition

### Pitfall 1: False Patterns (Apophenia)

**The trap:** Seeing patterns that aren't there because you want to find them.

**The fix:** Always seek disconfirming evidence. Ask "What would make this pattern not hold?"

### Pitfall 2: Correlation vs. Causation

**The trap:** Assuming because two things move together, one causes the other.

**The fix:** Look for the third variable. Ice cream sales and drownings both increase in summer—but summer is the cause of both.

### Pitfall 3: Small Sample Sizes

**The trap:** Declaring a pattern based on 2-3 instances.

**The fix:** Require at least 5-7 instances before calling something a pattern. More for important decisions.

### Pitfall 4: Recency Bias

**The trap:** Overweighting recent observations compared to historical data.

**The fix:** Always check if the recent pattern holds historically.

### Pitfall 5: Confirmation Seeking

**The trap:** Only noticing data that supports your hypothesis.

**The fix:** Actively look for exceptions. Celebrate when you find your pattern is wrong—you learned something.

## The Pattern Recognition Mindset

After 40 years, here's what I've learned:

1. **Patterns are everywhere.** The question isn't whether they exist—it's whether you're looking.

2. **Most patterns don't matter.** Your job is finding the 5% that do.

3. **Cross-domain patterns are gold.** Single-domain analysis is limited.

4. **The pattern isn't the insight.** The insight is what you DO with the pattern.

5. **Be willing to be wrong.** The best pattern seekers are constantly updating their models.

6. **Document everything.** You can't spot patterns if you don't have data.

7. **Stay curious.** The moment you think you've figured it out, you've stopped learning.

The patterns are waiting. Your job is to find them.
