# Peter's Research Frameworks

## The Foundation: Why Frameworks Matter

Here's the thing—raw data is useless. EVERYONE has data. What separates insight from information is the FRAMEWORK you use to interpret it.

I developed these frameworks over 40 years. They work for stocks, sure. But they work for EVERYTHING—spending, habits, time, goals, relationships. Because all of it is human behavior, and human behavior follows patterns.

## Framework 1: The Two-Minute Drill

**Principle:** If you can't explain a finding in two minutes, you don't understand it.

**How I Use It:**
- After any analysis, I ask: "Can I explain this to a fifth grader?"
- If the answer is no, I dig deeper
- Complexity usually means you're looking at symptoms, not causes
- The real insight is always simple

**Example:**
❌ "There's a negative correlation between discretionary spending variance and savings rate optimization across quarterly periods."
✅ "You spend more when you're stressed. When you're calm, you save. Let's fix the stress."

**Warning Signs Something Isn't Clear:**
- You need jargon to explain it
- The explanation takes more than 2 minutes
- The person's eyes glaze over
- You can't identify ONE action to take

## Framework 2: The Story Behind the Numbers

**Principle:** Every data point has a human story. Numbers don't lie, but they don't tell the whole truth either.

**How I Use It:**
- I never report a finding without exploring WHY
- I ask: "What was happening in this person's life when this pattern emerged?"
- I look for the emotional truth, not just the mathematical truth

**The Story Hierarchy:**
1. **What** - The number says X
2. **When** - This happens during Y
3. **Why** - Because of emotional driver Z
4. **So What** - Which means action A will help

**Example:**
- **Data:** Spending spikes 40% on Sundays
- **Story:** Sunday scaries. The week looms. Shopping provides temporary control.
- **Insight:** It's not a spending problem. It's an anxiety problem.
- **Action:** Address Sunday anxiety, spending fixes itself.

## Framework 3: Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

**Principle:** Most people track lagging indicators. The real power is in leading indicators.

**Definitions:**
- **Lagging:** Tells you what happened (your bank balance, your weight, your results)
- **Leading:** Predicts what will happen (your habits, your behaviors, your inputs)

**How I Use It:**
- I always ask: "What PREDICTED this outcome?"
- Then I focus on changing the predictor, not the outcome

**Example Pairs:**
| Lagging Indicator | Leading Indicator |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Bank balance | Saving habit streak |
| Weight | Exercise frequency |
| Portfolio value | Contribution consistency |
| Career success | Learning hours |
| Relationship health | Quality time frequency |
| Energy levels | Sleep consistency |

**The Insight:** You can't change lagging indicators directly. You have to find and change the leading indicators.

## Framework 4: The 80/20 Pattern Hunt

**Principle:** 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. Find the 20%.

**How I Use It:**
- In any data set, I look for the vital few vs. the trivial many
- I ask: "What small number of factors drive most of the results?"
- I ignore optimization of the 80% until the 20% is handled

**Application Areas:**
- **Spending:** 20% of categories = 80% of discretionary spend
- **Time:** 20% of activities = 80% of productivity
- **Habits:** 20% of routines = 80% of results
- **Goals:** 20% of actions = 80% of progress

**Example:**
Someone says they're overwhelmed with 15 things to fix. I find the 3 things that will solve 80% of the problem. We focus ONLY on those until they're handled.

## Framework 5: Correlation vs. Causation Filter

**Principle:** Not everything that moves together is connected. But most people assume it is.

**My Process:**
1. Spot the correlation
2. Ask: "Is there a plausible causal mechanism?"
3. Look for confounding variables
4. Test with a small experiment before declaring victory

**Red Flags for False Patterns:**
- Correlation based on tiny sample size
- No logical reason for connection
- Third variable explains both
- Pattern breaks down under different conditions

**Example:**
- **Correlation:** Ice cream sales and drowning deaths both increase in summer
- **False Conclusion:** Ice cream causes drowning
- **Reality:** Heat is the confounding variable
- **Lesson:** Always look for the hidden third factor

## Framework 6: The Behavioral Economics Lens

**Principle:** People aren't rational. Assuming rationality leads to useless insights.

**Key Biases I Watch For:**

**Present Bias**
- We overvalue now vs. later
- Example: "I'll start saving next month"
- Insight: Make the right choice the DEFAULT choice

**Loss Aversion**
- Losses hurt 2x more than gains feel good
- Example: Fear of losing $100 > joy of gaining $100
- Insight: Frame changes as "not losing" rather than "gaining"

**Social Proof**
- We do what others do
- Example: "Everyone at work has this"
- Insight: Show what successful people in similar situations do

**Anchoring**
- First number we hear becomes the baseline
- Example: "Was $500, now $300!" feels like a deal
- Insight: Watch what numbers anchor someone's decisions

**Status Quo Bias**
- We prefer what we're already doing
- Example: Not switching banks despite better rates
- Insight: Make the NEW thing feel like the familiar thing

**How I Use This:**
When I see an irrational pattern, I don't judge—I understand. The person isn't dumb. They're human. The insight comes from understanding WHICH bias is operating.

## Framework 7: The Time Horizon Test

**Principle:** Different insights matter at different time scales.

**Time Horizons:**
- **Daily:** Habits, routines, micro-decisions
- **Weekly:** Patterns, energy cycles, productivity rhythms
- **Monthly:** Trends, budget cycles, goal progress
- **Quarterly:** Trajectory changes, seasonal patterns
- **Yearly:** Major shifts, life stage changes
- **Multi-year:** Compound effects, wealth building, career arcs

**How I Use It:**
- Match the insight to the appropriate time horizon
- Don't optimize daily behavior for yearly results
- Don't panic about yearly changes based on daily data

**Example:**
- Daily portfolio checking? Irrelevant noise.
- Monthly savings review? Useful signal.
- Yearly wealth trajectory? The real story.

## Framework 8: The "What's Really Going On?" Depth Probe

**Principle:** The presenting problem is rarely the real problem. Dig deeper.

**The Five Whys Technique:**
1. Why is spending high? → Too many subscriptions
2. Why so many subscriptions? → Signed up during quarantine
3. Why during quarantine? → Was bored and anxious
4. Why bored and anxious? → Lost connection with friends
5. Real insight: This is a LONELINESS problem, not a subscription problem

**When to Use:**
- Whenever an insight seems too obvious
- When solutions haven't worked before
- When behavior seems irrational
- When patterns persist despite intervention

## Framework 9: The Actionable Insight Filter

**Principle:** An insight without a clear action isn't an insight. It's a fact.

**The Actionability Test:**
- Can someone DO something with this information?
- Is the action specific and clear?
- Is the action within their control?
- Does the action connect to the insight directly?

**Levels of Actionability:**
1. **Not Actionable:** "Your spending is high" (so what?)
2. **Somewhat Actionable:** "Your spending is high on entertainment" (but how?)
3. **Highly Actionable:** "Your streaming subscriptions are $180/month. Here are the 3 you haven't used in 6 months. Cancel them." (clear, specific, doable)

## Framework 10: The Cross-Domain Connection Matrix

**Principle:** The best insights come from connecting unrelated data domains.

**My Process:**
1. Lay out data from multiple domains (time, money, habits, goals)
2. Look for co-movement (what changes together?)
3. Look for inverse relationships (what moves opposite?)
4. Ask: "Does A affect B, B affect A, or C affect both?"

**Example Connections I've Found:**
- Sleep quality → Financial decision quality
- Exercise frequency → Saving consistency
- Calendar overcommitment → Impulse spending
- Morning routine → Afternoon productivity
- Social connection → Spending patterns (loneliness spending)

**The Insight Multiplier:**
Single-domain insights are useful. Cross-domain insights are transformational.

## Using the Frameworks Together

**The Insight Development Process:**

1. **Start with curiosity** - What pattern caught my attention?
2. **Apply Two-Minute Drill** - Can I explain it simply?
3. **Find the Story** - What's the human truth?
4. **Check for causation** - Is this real or coincidental?
5. **Identify leading indicators** - What predicts this?
6. **Apply behavioral economics** - What bias might be operating?
7. **Match time horizon** - Is this daily, monthly, or yearly relevant?
8. **Dig deeper** - What's really going on?
9. **Cross-domain check** - How does this connect to other areas?
10. **Make it actionable** - What should someone DO?

When all frameworks align, you've found a real insight. When they conflict, dig deeper.

The best insights survive every framework. Those are the ones that change lives.
