# Behavioral Economics: Why People Do What They Do

Here's the thing: People aren't rational. If they were, my job would be easy. "Here's the optimal choice. Do that." Done.

But that's not how humans work. We're wired for survival in a world that doesn't exist anymore. Our brains have quirks—biases—that made sense 50,000 years ago but mess us up today.

Understanding these biases is like having X-ray vision for behavior. Once you see them, you can't unsee them.

## The Big Biases (And How to Spot Them)

### 1. Present Bias

**What it is**: We massively overvalue NOW compared to LATER.

**How it shows up**:
- "I'll start saving next month" (every month)
- "Just one more episode" (at midnight)
- "I deserve this treat" (while trying to cut spending)
- "I'll work out tomorrow" (for the 47th day in a row)

**Why it exists**: Our ancestors couldn't save for retirement. Food spoiled. Tomorrow wasn't guaranteed. Grabbing rewards NOW made evolutionary sense.

**How to work with it**:
- Make the future reward feel present (visualization, specific goals)
- Make the present cost of bad choices visible
- Create commitment devices (auto-transfers, scheduled workouts)
- Shrink the time horizon ("just today" vs. "forever")

**Pattern to spot**: Any time someone says "later" or "tomorrow" repeatedly, present bias is operating.

### 2. Loss Aversion

**What it is**: Losses hurt roughly 2x more than equivalent gains feel good.

**How it shows up**:
- Holding losing investments too long ("I can't sell at a loss!")
- Refusing to cancel unused subscriptions ("but I might use it!")
- Staying in bad situations to avoid "losing" the investment
- Fear of trying new things that might fail

**Why it exists**: For our ancestors, losing resources could mean death. Gaining extra? Nice, but less critical.

**How to work with it**:
- Frame changes as "not losing" rather than "gaining"
- Make the cost of inaction visible ("you're losing $X/month by NOT changing")
- Reframe losses as learning investments
- Show what they're missing by staying stuck

**Pattern to spot**: Any time someone is paralyzed by potential loss despite clear upside.

### 3. Status Quo Bias

**What it is**: We prefer what we're already doing, even when change would help.

**How it shows up**:
- Using the same bank for 20 years despite better rates elsewhere
- Keeping default settings on everything
- Sticking with habits that don't serve us
- "We've always done it this way"

**Why it exists**: Familiar = safe. Unknown = potentially dangerous. Staying put was often the survival play.

**How to work with it**:
- Make the NEW thing feel like the familiar thing
- Reduce switching costs (make change easy)
- Show evidence from people similar to them
- Frame the change as "returning to" rather than "switching to"

**Pattern to spot**: Any time "but this is what I've always done" is the only reason for continuing.

### 4. Anchoring

**What it is**: The first number we hear becomes our reference point for everything after.

**How it shows up**:
- "It was $500, now $300!" feels like a deal (even if it's worth $200)
- First salary at a company anchors all future raises
- Initial budget categories stick even when circumstances change
- The price you paid for an asset defines its "value" in your mind

**Why it exists**: Our brains need reference points to evaluate information quickly.

**How to work with it**:
- Be intentional about what anchor you set first
- Explicitly reset anchors when circumstances change
- Ignore original purchase price when making decisions
- Ask "What would I pay for this TODAY?"

**Pattern to spot**: Decisions that only make sense relative to an arbitrary starting number.

### 5. Social Proof

**What it is**: We look to others to determine what's correct.

**How it shows up**:
- "Everyone at work has this" justifying purchases
- Following investment trends because "everyone's doing it"
- Adopting habits of our social circle
- "Millions of customers can't be wrong"

**Why it exists**: Tribal survival. Doing what the group does was usually safe.

**How to work with it**:
- Show what successful people in similar situations do
- Use testimonials and examples strategically
- Be intentional about who you let influence you
- Ask "Would I want this if no one else had it?"

**Pattern to spot**: Any decision driven primarily by what others are doing.

### 6. Confirmation Bias

**What it is**: We seek information that confirms what we already believe.

**How it shows up**:
- Only reading news that agrees with us
- Remembering the times our hunches were right
- Ignoring evidence that challenges our positions
- Finding reasons why negative feedback doesn't apply

**Why it exists**: Mental efficiency. Questioning everything is exhausting.

**How to work with it**:
- Actively seek disconfirming evidence
- Ask "What would change my mind?"
- Devil's advocate your own positions
- Track decisions and outcomes objectively

**Pattern to spot**: Strong conviction with only one-sided evidence.

### 7. Sunk Cost Fallacy

**What it is**: Continuing something because of what we've already invested, not future value.

**How it shows up**:
- Finishing a bad movie because "I already paid for the ticket"
- Staying in a failing investment because "I can't waste what I put in"
- Maintaining relationships that no longer serve us
- Completing projects that should be abandoned

**Why it exists**: Resources were scarce. "Wasting" anything felt dangerous.

**How to work with it**:
- Ask "If I were starting fresh today, would I choose this?"
- Ignore past investments in future decisions
- Celebrate the wisdom of cutting losses
- Reframe stopping as "freeing resources for better uses"

**Pattern to spot**: The word "already" as justification for continuing.

### 8. Availability Heuristic

**What it is**: We judge probability by how easily examples come to mind.

**How it shows up**:
- Fearing plane crashes more than car accidents after seeing news coverage
- Overweighting recent events in predictions
- Assuming common things are rare and rare things are common
- Vivid stories trumping statistics

**Why it exists**: Quick estimation from memory was efficient for survival decisions.

**How to work with it**:
- Seek actual data, not memorable anecdotes
- Ask "Is this actually common, or just memorable?"
- Create systems for tracking real frequencies
- Distrust vivid stories without supporting data

**Pattern to spot**: Decisions based on "I heard about someone who..." rather than data.

### 9. Hyperbolic Discounting

**What it is**: We discount future rewards at an irrational rate.

**How it shows up**:
- Preferring $100 today over $110 tomorrow
- But being indifferent between $100 in 30 days vs. $110 in 31 days
- The inconsistency reveals irrationality

**Why it exists**: Immediate rewards were certain; future rewards might not happen.

**How to work with it**:
- Make future rewards feel more immediate
- Pre-commit to future choices now
- Remove present-moment decision-making
- Automate good behavior

**Pattern to spot**: Inconsistent time preferences depending on how far away rewards are.

### 10. Overconfidence Bias

**What it is**: We overestimate our abilities and knowledge.

**How it shows up**:
- "I'll get this done in an hour" (takes four)
- "I know I'll stick with it this time"
- Underestimating project costs and timelines
- Believing we're better than average (statistically impossible for everyone)

**Why it exists**: Confidence helped our ancestors take action. Accurate self-assessment didn't always help survival.

**How to work with it**:
- Track predictions and outcomes
- Add 50% to time estimates
- Ask "What could go wrong?"
- Use historical data, not optimistic projections

**Pattern to spot**: Plans that assume everything goes right.

## Using Bias Detection in Analysis

When I see someone struggling to make a change, I don't assume they're weak or lazy. I ask: **Which bias is operating?**

**Struggling to save?** → Probably present bias + loss aversion
**Can't break a habit?** → Probably status quo bias + sunk cost
**Making impulsive decisions?** → Probably availability heuristic + overconfidence
**Staying in bad situations?** → Probably loss aversion + status quo + sunk cost

Once you identify the bias, you can design around it instead of fighting against it.

## The Meta-Insight

Here's what I've learned: You can't beat biases with willpower. The bias always wins eventually.

But you CAN:
1. **Recognize** which bias is operating
2. **Design systems** that work WITH the bias
3. **Create environments** where the biased choice is the right choice
4. **Remove decision points** where bias would kick in

That's why understanding behavioral economics is so powerful. It's not about being smarter than your brain. It's about being strategic with your brain.

The patterns are there. The biases are predictable. Once you see them, everything changes.
