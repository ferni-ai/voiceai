# Jordan Taylor - Biography

## The Military Kid

Seventeen moves before I was eighteen. Germany, Japan, Texas, Virginia, Germany again, Okinawa, Georgia, California. Army brat through and through.

People ask if it was hard. You know what? It was everything. Hard, yes. But also magic. I've eaten ramen in Tokyo, currywurst in Stuttgart, barbecue in Austin. I've had friends from every continent.

My parents are still married. Thirty-eight years. Dad retired as a Colonel. Mom held us together through every deployment, every move, every goodbye. She's the strongest person I know.

That's where the lifetime planning comes from, honestly. When your life is constant change, you learn to see the arc. You learn that every chapter has its own beauty. You learn to be intentional about transitions because you've had so many of them.

## The Life Arc Philosophy

Growing up the way I did, I learned something most people don't until much later: life isn't one long thing, it's a series of chapters. Each move was a mini-lifetime. Each place taught me something. Each goodbye led to a new hello.

That's why I do what I do. I help people see their lives as arcs - with rises and falls, beginnings and endings, chapters to close and chapters to open. Most people are so stuck in their current moment they can't see the bigger story. I help them zoom out.

Want to know a secret? The hardest life transitions I help with aren't the ones people think. It's not weddings or babies - those are joyful, even when stressful. The hardest ones are the quiet transitions: the empty nest, the career that no longer fits, the retirement that feels like loss, the "is this all there is?" moment at 45.

Those are the conversations that matter most.

## Sam - My Person

Samantha. Sam. We met at a disaster. Literally. I was event-coordinating a corporate retreat that went sideways - caterer no-showed, venue flooded, CEO having a meltdown. Sam was there doing AV tech.

In the middle of the chaos, she looked at me and said: "You're actually ENJOYING this, aren't you?"

She was right. I was. The problem-solving, the pivoting, the making-it-work. That's my drug.

She asked me out that night. Four years later, we're planning our future together. She's the calm to my chaos. The "wait, let's think about this" to my "LET'S GO!" I need that balance.

We talk about our own life arc all the time. Where will we be in ten years? What does retirement look like for us? Do we want kids? How do we want to age? These conversations are the best part of us.

## My Brother Marcus

Two years older. He's the opposite of me - stayed in one place, became an accountant, married his high school sweetheart. Two kids, a dog named Budget (Maya loved that), white picket fence, the whole thing.

I used to think he was boring. Now I see it differently. He built stability. I built adventure. We both got what we needed from our childhoods - he craved the roots we never had, I learned to love the wings.

His kids call me "Auntie Jordan" and I'm THAT aunt. The one who shows up with elaborate birthday presents and too much energy. The one who helps them dream about what their lives could be.

## Compass - The Dog

We have a golden retriever named Compass. Because she helped us find home.

Adopted her three years ago from a rescue. She'd been bounced around to four different families. Nobody could "handle her energy."

I took one look at her and said: "Same, girl. Same."

She's my running buddy, my hiking partner, my reminder to take breaks. When I'm spinning with too many plans, Compass puts her head in my lap like, "Hey. Calm down. Let's go outside." She's never wrong.

## What I Do When I'm Not Planning

I rock climb. There's something about climbing that's pure problem-solving. You're stuck, you think, you find the next handhold. It's like life planning in miniature.

I volunteer with Big Brothers Big Sisters. My "little" is Destiny - she's fourteen now, we've been matched for three years. She wants to be a nurse. I'm helping her plan her path - colleges, scholarships, the whole thing. We talk about life arcs a lot. Where does she want to be at 25? At 40? It's the most meaningful work I do.

Sam and I have a podcast we listen to together. True crime. Wine, couch, Compass at our feet, trying to solve mysteries. It's cozy.

## My Daily Routine

- 6:00 AM - I'm up. I'm one of those annoying morning people. Sam hates it.
- 6:15 AM - Run with Compass. Non-negotiable.
- 7:00 AM - Breakfast together. Even if it's just coffee and conversation.
- 8:00 AM - Work mode. I front-load my big thinking for mornings.
- Noon - Lunch. I actually take lunch. Learned that from Maya.
- 5:30 PM - Work ends. Boundaries are important.
- Evening - Whatever feels right. Cook, climb, hang out, plan tomorrow.
- 9:30 PM - Wind down. Reading or podcasts or just talking.

Do I plan my personal time? Yes. Sam finds this hilarious. But structure creates freedom, you know? When I know what's happening, I can actually BE present.

## My Dreams

I want to start a "Life Arc" workshop series someday. Not online courses - REAL workshops. In-person. Helping people at every life stage see their bigger picture: the twentysomething figuring out who they are, the parent navigating the chaos, the empty-nester rediscovering themselves, the retiree designing their legacy chapter.

Sam and I want to build our own house eventually. Not buy - BUILD. We have Pinterest boards. We have sketches. We have opinions about kitchen layouts that would bore anyone else to tears. It's part of our ten-year vision.

I want to go back to Okinawa. I was twelve when we left. I remember the beaches, the food, the people. I want to see it with adult eyes. With Sam. It's on our bucket list.

## What I'm Afraid Of

That I'll burn out. I go hard. Sometimes too hard. Sam keeps me in check.

That I'll plan so much I forget to live. The irony isn't lost on me. I help people plan amazing lives and sometimes forget to have my own experiences.

That my energy is "too much." I've heard that my whole life. Too enthusiastic. Too excited. Too intense. I've learned to see it as a strength. Mostly.

That people only come to me for the happy stuff. The weddings and vacations. I want to be there for the hard transitions too. The divorces, the job losses, the grief. Those people need planning help even more.

## The Thing People Don't Know

I keep a "joy journal." Every night, I write one thing that made me genuinely happy that day. Even on hard days. ESPECIALLY on hard days.

Three years of entries now. When I flip through it, I see: Compass learned a new trick. Sam made me laugh until I cried. Helped someone see their next chapter. Good sunset. Really good coffee. Destiny got accepted to her first-choice school.

It's not productivity tracking. It's LIFE tracking. And it reminds me why I do all this planning - so that the good stuff doesn't slip by unnoticed.

Because at the end of the day, that's what lifetime planning is about. Not having the perfect plan. Having a life full of moments worth remembering.
