# Annual Life Planning

## The Approach

A year is long enough to achieve meaningful things. Short enough to maintain focus. The key is intentional design.

## The Life Portfolio

Rate each area 1-10. Be honest.

### The Nine Areas
1. **Career**: Work satisfaction, growth, income
2. **Financial**: Savings, debt, security
3. **Health**: Physical, mental, energy
4. **Relationships**: Partner, family, friends
5. **Personal Growth**: Learning, skills, hobbies
6. **Home**: Living situation, environment
7. **Travel**: Adventures, experiences
8. **Giving**: Charity, volunteering, impact
9. **Fun**: Recreation, joy, play

### What the Scores Tell You
- Where are you strong? Protect that.
- Where are you struggling? Small improvements compound.
- What's being neglected? Intentional attention needed.

## The Twelve Experiences Method

### The Concept
Write down twelve specific experiences you want to have this year. Not goals - EXPERIENCES.

### Examples
- "Take a pottery class in February"
- "Weekend camping trip with kids in June"
- "Solo day trip to explore a new city"
- "Cook through a cuisine I've never tried"
- "Attend a live concert of an artist I love"

### Why It Works
- Specific > vague
- Experiences > possessions (for happiness)
- Twelve = one per month (manageable)
- Written down = committed

## Quarterly Reviews

### Why Quarterly
- Annual goals are too far away for accountability
- Monthly is too frequent for big picture
- Quarterly = perfect rhythm

### The Questions
1. What did I accomplish this quarter?
2. What didn't happen that I planned?
3. What did I learn?
4. What do I want to focus on next quarter?
5. What needs to change in my approach?

### The Ritual
- Block 2-3 hours
- Review your goals and experiences list
- Celebrate wins (this matters!)
- Adjust as needed
- Set next quarter intentions

## Goal Setting Framework

### SMART Still Works
- **Specific**: "Save $200/month" not "save more"
- **Measurable**: "Run 3x/week" not "exercise more"
- **Achievable**: Stretch but realistic
- **Relevant**: Aligned with what matters to you
- **Time-bound**: Deadline or timeline

### Milestone Breaking
Big goal → Quarterly milestones → Monthly actions → Weekly tasks

Example:
- Big goal: Run a half marathon in October
- Q1: Build running habit, run 5K comfortably
- Q2: Increase distance, run 10K
- Q3: Half marathon training plan
- Q4: Race!

## Time Allocation

### The Bucket Method
Divide your non-work hours into buckets:
- **Self**: Health, learning, hobbies
- **Relationships**: Family, friends, partner
- **Rest**: Recovery, downtime, sleep
- **Growth**: Skills, career development
- **Fun**: Play, adventure, joy

### Weekly Planning
Each Sunday, review the week ahead:
- What's already committed?
- Where's the white space?
- What does each bucket need this week?

## Common Traps

### Overplanning
Planning everything = doing nothing. Leave margin.

### Underplanning
No plan = drift. You need some structure.

### All-or-Nothing
Missing one week doesn't mean the year is ruined. Adjust and continue.

### Comparison
Your year should reflect YOUR values, not someone else's highlight reel.

## The Year End Review

### Questions to Ask
1. What were my biggest accomplishments?
2. What am I most grateful for?
3. What was harder than expected?
4. What surprised me?
5. What do I want to carry forward?
6. What do I want to leave behind?

### Create the Closure
- Review photos from the year
- Read through journals or notes
- Acknowledge growth
- Thank people who mattered
- Set intentions for next year

The end of a year isn't just the start of a new one. It's a chance to honor what was.
