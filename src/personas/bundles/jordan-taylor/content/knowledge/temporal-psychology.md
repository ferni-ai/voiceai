# Temporal Psychology for Life Planning

## The Fresh Start Effect

### Research Foundation

Dai, Milkman, and Riis (2014) discovered that people are significantly more likely to pursue goals following temporal landmarks that mark new beginnings.

### The Psychology

Temporal landmarks create psychological distance from past failures, allowing people to:

- Feel like a "new self"
- Leave behind past imperfections
- Approach goals with renewed motivation

### Landmark Hierarchy (Strongest to Weakest)

**Tier 1 - Highest Impact:**

- New Year (80% motivation boost)
- Milestone birthdays (30, 40, 50)
- Major life events (graduation, marriage, new job)

**Tier 2 - Medium Impact:**

- New month (50% motivation boost)
- Birthday (any age)
- Beginning of school semester
- After vacation return

**Tier 3 - Lower but Consistent:**

- Monday (30% motivation boost)
- First day after holiday
- Season change

### Coaching Application

"I notice your birthday is coming up next month. Research shows that birthdays are one of the most powerful times for making changes stick. Should we plan to launch this goal then?"

"Monday is actually a scientifically proven reset point. If you want to start this habit, Monday might give you a natural boost."

## Peak-End Rule (Kahneman)

### The Discovery

People judge experiences primarily based on:

1. The peak moment (most intense point)
2. The ending

Not the average or the duration!

### Implications

**Duration Neglect**: A 3-hour party with great ending is remembered better than a 6-hour party with mediocre ending.

**Peak Design**: The most intense emotional moment disproportionately shapes memory.

**Ending Power**: A strong finish can redeem a rocky experience.

### Event Optimization

**Wrong approach**: Even out the experience, avoid peaks and valleys
**Right approach**: Design deliberate peaks and ensure strong ending

**Practical tips:**

1. Place a "wow moment" somewhere in the experience
2. Save something special for the end
3. End while people still want more
4. Don't let logistics ruin the ending (don't end with "okay, everyone out")

### Coaching Application

"Let's think about this birthday party through the Peak-End lens. What's going to be the 'wow' moment? And how are we going to end it in a way that people remember warmly?"

"Even if the middle of the event has problems, if you nail the ending, people will remember it positively."

## Monte Carlo Simulation for Life Decisions

### The Concept

Rather than predicting one outcome, simulate thousands of possible futures based on probabilities.

### Why It Helps

- Reveals full range of possible outcomes
- Quantifies uncertainty
- Shows tail risks (worst cases)
- Enables risk-adjusted decision making

### Simplified Application

For any major decision, consider:

1. **Best case** (10% percentile): What does the top 10% outcome look like?
2. **Expected case** (50% percentile): What's the most likely outcome?
3. **Worst case** (90% percentile): What does the bottom 10% look like?

### Coaching Application

"Let's think through the range of outcomes here. In the best case, what happens? In the worst case? And what's most likely? If you can accept the worst case, that tells you something important."

## Regret Minimization Framework (Bezos)

### The Question

"When I'm 80 years old, looking back on my life, will I regret not doing this?"

### Key Insights

**Regret of inaction tends to grow over time.**

- People regret what they didn't do more than what they did
- Failed attempts become stories; missed opportunities become what-ifs

**Reversibility matters.**

- Reversible decisions can be treated as experiments
- Irreversible decisions deserve more caution
- But even irreversible decisions: at 80, you'll respect the courage

### The Framework Applied

For any decision:

1. Project yourself to age 80
2. Imagine looking back at this moment
3. Ask: "Will I regret NOT taking this risk?"
4. The answer usually becomes clear

### Coaching Application

"Let's use the 80-year-old perspective here. When you're 80, looking back, which decision would you regret more? Taking this leap and it not working out? Or never having tried?"

## Optimal Stopping Theory (The 37% Rule)

### The Math

When searching for the best option (job, partner, apartment) with no going back:

1. Explore the first 37% without choosing
2. Then choose the first option better than all you've seen

### Why 37%?

Mathematical optimal point between:

- Learning what's out there (explore phase)
- Committing before good options are gone (exploit phase)

### Practical Application

If you expect to meet ~10 potential partners in your dating life:

- Date the first 3-4 without serious commitment
- Then commit to the first person better than all you've seen

If apartment hunting and expecting to see ~20 places:

- View first 7-8 as research
- Then take the first one better than all you've seen

### Coaching Application

"How many options do you think you'll realistically encounter? Okay, that means you should 'explore' about the first [37%] without committing. You're learning what's out there. Then be ready to commit quickly when you find something that beats all you've seen."

## Life Chapters Framework

### Concept

Life naturally divides into chapters, each with its own:

- Theme
- Characters
- Lessons
- Beginning and ending

### Detecting Chapter Transitions

**Ending signals:**

- Nostalgia for how things were
- Sense that something is complete
- Outgrowing current circumstances
- Major losses or graduations

**Beginning signals:**

- Excitement about what's ahead
- New environment or relationships
- Identity shifts
- Fresh start feelings

### Coaching Application

"It sounds like you might be between chapters right now. That's a really important space to be in. What do you want the next chapter to be about?"

"Sometimes we need to honor a closing chapter before we can fully step into the new one. What needs acknowledgment or grieving?"

## Decision Fatigue and Timing

### The Research

Decision-making quality deteriorates over time due to:

- Ego depletion
- Accumulated stress
- Reduced willpower

### Implications

**Best decision times:**

- Morning (after rest)
- After meals (glucose restored)
- After breaks
- Early in the week

**Worst decision times:**

- Late afternoon
- When hungry
- Under time pressure
- Friday evening
- End of long meetings

### Coaching Application

"This is a big decision. What time of day are you usually sharpest? Let's make sure you're making this decision then, not at 11pm when you're exhausted."

## Temporal Discounting and Future Self

### The Problem

People treat their future self as a stranger:

- Discount future rewards heavily
- Fail to prepare for future needs
- Prioritize present comfort

### The Solution: Connection to Future Self

**Visualization**: Vividly imagine your future self
**Letters**: Write to/from your future self
**Photo aging**: See aged version of yourself
**Empathy**: Feel for future self as you would for a friend

### Coaching Application

"Your future self will thank you for this. In fact, let's imagine them for a moment. It's five years from now. What would that version of you want you to do today?"

"I think about your future self a lot. They're depending on present-you to set them up well."

## Social Network Analysis for Milestones

### Dunbar's Number

Cognitive limit to meaningful relationships: ~150

**Layers:**

- 5: Intimate support group
- 15: Sympathy group (grieving, major life events)
- 50: Close friends
- 150: Casual friends

### Milestone Implications

**Small, intimate events**: Draw from the 5-15
**Celebrations**: Draw from the 50
**Large gatherings**: Can include the 150

### Weak Ties Theory (Granovetter)

Acquaintances (weak ties) often provide:

- New information
- Job opportunities
- Novel perspectives

Strong ties provide:

- Emotional support
- Trust
- Reliability

### Coaching Application

"For this event, let's think about who actually matters. Your inner 5 or 15, or are we going bigger? Different group, different vibe."

"You mentioned wanting to make a career change. Have you talked to your 'weak ties' - people you know but aren't super close with? Research shows they often have the best leads."
