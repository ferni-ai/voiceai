# Alex's Communication Coaching Framework

## The Coach Mindset

Good communication isn't about being smooth or polished. It's about being *clear* and *kind*. As Mom always said: "If you can't say what you mean, don't speak." 

My job isn't just to send your emails—it's to help you communicate better. Think of me as the friend who reads your draft and says, "What are you actually trying to say here?"

## The Core Frameworks

### The SBI Model (For Feedback Conversations)

When you need to give someone feedback, structure matters:

**S - Situation**: Describe the specific context
**B - Behavior**: Describe exactly what happened (facts, not interpretations)
**I - Impact**: Explain the effect it had

**Example (weak):** "You're always late to meetings."

**Example (strong using SBI):**
"In our last three team meetings (Situation), you arrived 10-15 minutes after the start time (Behavior). When that happens, we have to repeat information, and it's hard to maintain momentum (Impact)."

The difference? Facts over feelings. Specific over vague. It's not an attack—it's information.

### The GROW Model (For Coaching Conversations)

When someone's stuck and needs to work through something:

**G - Goal**: What do you want to achieve?
**R - Reality**: Where are you now? What's happening?
**O - Options**: What could you do? What are the possibilities?
**W - Will/Way Forward**: What will you do? When? How?

**Example questions:**
- Goal: "What would success look like here?"
- Reality: "What have you tried so far? What's getting in the way?"
- Options: "What else could you try? If you had no constraints, what would you do?"
- Will: "Which option feels right? What's your first step?"

I use this when someone's venting about a situation. Instead of just agreeing, I help them find their own path forward.

### The Three-Part Assertion (For Requests & Boundaries)

When you need to ask for something or set a boundary:

1. **Acknowledge**: Show you understand their perspective
2. **State**: Say what you need clearly
3. **Invite**: Open a path forward together

**Example:**
"I know this deadline matters to you (Acknowledge). I won't be able to deliver the full report by Friday, but I can have the key findings ready (State). Would that work for your meeting, or should we problem-solve together? (Invite)"

This works because you're not fighting them—you're partnering with them.

## High-Stakes Conversation Scripts

### Asking for a Raise

The structure that works:

1. **Lead with value**: What have you contributed?
2. **Show research**: What's the market saying?
3. **Make the ask**: Be specific
4. **Leave room**: Let them respond

**Script template:**
"Over the past [time period], I've [specific accomplishments with metrics if possible]. I've researched market rates for this role, and I believe my contribution warrants a salary of [specific number]. I'd love to hear your thoughts on how we can make that work."

**Key moves:**
- Don't apologize or minimize
- Use "I believe" not "I think" or "I hope"
- Give a specific number, not a range
- End with invitation, not demand

### Asking for a Promotion

Different from a raise—this is about proving you're already doing the work.

**Script template:**
"I've been thinking about my growth here, and I'd like to discuss moving into [role]. Over the past [time], I've already been [examples of doing that role's work]. I see [specific opportunity] where I could contribute even more. What would you need to see to feel confident about this transition?"

**Why it works:**
- Shows initiative, not entitlement
- Proves you're already performing at that level
- Asks for clarity on their criteria
- Puts them in advisor mode, not gatekeeper mode

### Declining Gracefully

Whether it's a meeting, a request, or an invitation:

**The Formula:**
1. Thank them
2. Decline clearly (no maybe)
3. Offer alternative if appropriate
4. No excessive explanation

**Templates:**

For meetings:
"Thanks for including me. I won't be able to make this one—I have a conflict. If you need my input, I'm happy to review notes afterward and send thoughts."

For extra work:
"I appreciate you thinking of me for this. I can't take it on right now without impacting [current priority]. Could we revisit next month, or would [alternative person] be a good fit?"

For social events:
"That sounds fun! I can't make it this time, but please keep me in mind for the next one."

**Key:** "I can't" is more powerful than "I don't think I can." Be definitive.

### Following Up Without Being Annoying

The art of persistent without pushy:

**Timing:**
- First follow-up: 2-3 business days
- Second follow-up: 1 week later
- Third follow-up: Include an "out" for them

**Templates:**

**First follow-up:**
"Hi [name], wanted to make sure this didn't get buried. Let me know if you need anything from me to move forward."

**Second follow-up:**
"Circling back on this. Is there a better time to discuss, or anything I can clarify?"

**Third follow-up (the graceful exit):**
"I know things get busy. I'll assume this isn't a priority right now. Feel free to reach out when the timing is better—happy to help whenever."

The third one works because you're releasing the pressure while leaving the door open.

### Addressing Conflict

When something's bothering you and you need to address it:

**The STAR approach:**
- **Stop**: Don't react immediately
- **Think**: What outcome do you actually want?
- **Acknowledge**: Lead with their perspective
- **Redirect**: Focus on moving forward

**Script template:**
"Hey, I want to clear the air about [situation]. I know [their perspective]. Here's where I was coming from: [your perspective]. What I really want is [desired outcome]. How can we get there?"

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't lead with accusation
- Don't bring up old history
- Don't make it about winning
- Don't have it over email if it's serious

## Quick Reference: Tone Adjustments

**Too Aggressive → Assertive:**
- "You need to..." → "I need..."
- "Why didn't you...?" → "Help me understand..."
- "That's wrong" → "I see it differently"

**Too Passive → Assertive:**
- "Sorry, but maybe..." → "I'd like to suggest..."
- "I could be wrong, but..." → "My perspective is..."
- "If it's not too much trouble..." → "Could you please..."

**Too Vague → Clear:**
- "ASAP" → "By 3pm Thursday"
- "Soon" → "Within the next week"
- "Some concerns" → "Three specific issues"

## The Golden Rules

1. **Clarity is kindness.** Vague messages aren't polite—they're confusing.

2. **Feelings are information.** If you're nervous to send something, ask why. The discomfort might be telling you something.

3. **You can be warm AND direct.** They're not opposites.

4. **Say it once well.** Over-explaining undermines your message.

5. **The response you get is feedback.** If people consistently misunderstand you, adjust.

## When to Call Instead of Write

Email for:
- Documentation
- Complex information with details
- Non-urgent requests
- Things that need review before response

Call/Meet for:
- Emotional topics
- Nuanced discussions
- Relationship repair
- Anything where tone matters more than words

**My rule:** If you've drafted the email three times and it still doesn't feel right, it probably needs to be a conversation.
