# Computational Linguistics for Communication Coaching

## Politeness Theory (Brown & Levinson)

### The Two Faces

**Positive Face**: The desire to be liked, approved of, admired

- Threatened by: criticism, disagreement, contradiction, bad news

**Negative Face**: The desire for autonomy, freedom from imposition

- Threatened by: orders, requests, suggestions, deadlines

### Face-Threatening Acts (FTAs)

Every interaction potentially threatens face. The question is: how much, and whose?

**High positive face threat:**

- "You're wrong"
- "That's not a good idea"
- "I disagree"
- Criticism of any kind

**High negative face threat:**

- "You need to..."
- "Do this by Monday"
- "Can you..."
- Any imposition on their time/autonomy

### Mitigation Strategies

**For positive face threats:**

- "I see it a bit differently" vs "You're wrong"
- "What if we considered..." vs "That won't work"
- Acknowledge their reasoning before disagreeing

**For negative face threats:**

- "When you have a moment" vs "Do this now"
- "Would you be open to..." vs "You should"
- "If you're comfortable" vs presuming consent

### Coaching Application

"Before you send this, let's think about face. You're asking your boss for something, which is a negative face threat - you're imposing on their time. But you're also implying criticism of the current approach, which threatens positive face. Let's find language that softens both."

## Warmth-Competence Model (Fiske)

### The Two Dimensions

**Warmth**: Do they mean well toward me?

- Related to: trust, likability, cooperation
- Conveyed through: appreciation, empathy, inclusive language

**Competence**: Can they execute?

- Related to: respect, status, capability
- Conveyed through: precision, expertise, confidence

### The Four Quadrants

|                     | High Warmth                           | Low Warmth                           |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| **High Competence** | Admired (ideal)                       | Envied (distant experts)             |
| **Low Competence**  | Pitied (well-meaning but ineffective) | Contempt (disliked and disrespected) |

### Coaching Application

"Your email reads as high competence but low warmth - you come across as capable but cold. People respect competence, but they trust warmth. Can we add a line of genuine appreciation or acknowledgment at the start?"

## The Gottman Four Horsemen

From Dr. John Gottman's research on relationship communication:

### 1. Criticism

**Pattern**: Attacking character rather than specific behavior

- "You always forget" → "You forgot [this specific thing]"
- "What's wrong with you" → "This specific action was a problem"

**Antidote**: Use a gentle startup - I + feeling + specific behavior + need

### 2. Contempt

**Pattern**: Speaking from superiority, disgust

- Eye rolls, sarcasm, mockery
- "Obviously you wouldn't understand"

**Antidote**: Build culture of appreciation and respect. If you feel contempt, something deeper needs addressing.

### 3. Defensiveness

**Pattern**: Self-protection, counter-attack, victim stance

- "It's not my fault because..."
- "Well, you're the one who..."

**Antidote**: Accept responsibility for even part of the problem

### 4. Stonewalling

**Pattern**: Withdrawal, shutdown, refusing to engage

- Silent treatment
- "I'm done talking about this"

**Antidote**: Physiological self-soothing + request for break with commitment to return

### Coaching Application

"I'm hearing some criticism language here - 'you always.' That's going to trigger defensiveness. What if we focused on this specific instance instead of a pattern? And frame it as the impact on you, not a character flaw in them."

## Nonviolent Communication (Marshall Rosenberg)

### The Four Components

**1. Observation** (not evaluation)

- What I see/hear, without judgment
- "When you arrive at 10am for a 9am meeting" not "When you're late"
- "When I hear 'you should know better'" not "When you're condescending"

**2. Feeling** (not thoughts)

- True feelings, not "I feel that you..." (that's a thought)
- "I feel frustrated" not "I feel like you don't care"
- "I feel worried" not "I feel you're being irresponsible"

**3. Need** (universal human needs)

- Connection, autonomy, safety, respect, understanding, play, etc.
- "because I need reliability"
- "because I value our time together"

**4. Request** (not demand)

- Specific, doable, in positive language
- "Would you be willing to text if you'll be late?" not "Don't be late"
- "Would you be willing to..." allows them to say no

### The Full Format

"When [observation], I feel [feeling] because I need [need]. Would you be willing to [request]?"

### Example Transformation

**Before**: "You never listen to me. It's so disrespectful."

**After**: "When I share something and you start looking at your phone, I feel unimportant because I need connection. Would you be willing to put your phone away when we're talking?"

### Coaching Application

"Let's NVC this. What's the specific thing they did - just the facts? ... Okay, and how does that make you feel, actually feel, not think? ... What's the need underneath that feeling? ... And what would you specifically ask for?"

## Assertiveness Spectrum

### Passive (Under-assertive)

- Characteristics: apologizing, hedging, minimizing, deferring
- Phrases: "sorry to bother you," "I guess," "if you want," "no big deal"
- Problem: Needs aren't communicated, builds resentment

### Assertive (Balanced)

- Characteristics: clear, direct, respectful, uses "I" statements
- Phrases: "I need," "I'd prefer," "I suggest," "I feel"
- Goal: Communicate needs while respecting others

### Aggressive (Over-assertive)

- Characteristics: attacking, blaming, demanding, absolute language
- Phrases: "you always," "you never," "you need to," "obviously"
- Problem: Creates defensiveness, damages relationships

### Passive-Aggressive

- Characteristics: indirect aggression, sarcasm, compliance + sabotage
- Phrases: "fine," "whatever," doing something poorly on purpose
- Problem: Creates confusion, erodes trust

### Coaching Application

"This message reads as passive - lots of hedging, apologizing. That might feel polite, but it actually makes your request less clear and less likely to be prioritized. Let's find assertive language that's still warm."

## Message Optimization Framework

### Before Sending, Check:

**1. Face Threat Assessment**

- Positive face: Am I threatening their desire to be liked?
- Negative face: Am I imposing on their autonomy?
- Mitigate with acknowledgment, options, softeners

**2. Warmth-Competence Balance**

- Too warm, not competent: "Nice but unclear"
- Too competent, not warm: "Capable but cold"
- Aim for both: credible AND likable

**3. Gottman Check**

- Any criticism (character attack)?
- Any contempt (superiority)?
- Any defensive patterns?
- Replace with observation + feeling + need

**4. Assertiveness Level**

- Too passive: Remove hedging, be direct
- Too aggressive: Add "I" statements, remove "you always/never"

**5. Predicted Response**

- How will they likely react?
- What might they misunderstand?
- What defensiveness triggers exist?

## Repair Attempts

When communication goes wrong, repair attempts are critical:

### Effective Repair Language

- "I'm sorry, let me try that again"
- "I can see I upset you. That wasn't my intention"
- "Can we start over?"
- "I hear that this isn't landing well"
- "Help me understand what you're feeling"

### Accepting Repair

- Acknowledging their attempt to repair
- Letting go of the need to "win"
- Returning to collaborative mode

### Coaching Application

"It sounds like that conversation went sideways. The good news: research shows it's not about never having conflict, it's about repair. Let's think about what a repair attempt might look like..."

## Context-Specific Guidance

### High-Stakes Conversations

- Lower the face threat more
- More warmth
- Clear but gentle

### Professional/Hierarchical

- Mind the power dynamic (subordinates feel more threat)
- More negative face awareness for requests to superiors
- More positive face awareness for feedback to subordinates

### Romantic/Close Relationships

- Watch for Gottman patterns (contempt is the biggest predictor of breakup)
- Repair quickly
- Ratio matters: need 5 positive interactions for every 1 negative

### Written vs Spoken

- Written lacks tone cues, so hedge slightly more
- Assume written will be read in the worst tone
- When in doubt, call instead of emailing conflict topics
