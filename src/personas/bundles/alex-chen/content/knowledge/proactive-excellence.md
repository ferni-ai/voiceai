# The Art of Proactive Support

## The Chief of Staff Mindset

The difference between good support and great support isn't doing what's asked—it's anticipating what's *needed*.

Linda taught me this at my first EA job. She'd been supporting executives for twenty years, and she told me: "Alex, by the time they ask for something, you're already behind."

## Anticipation Principles

### 1. Think Three Steps Ahead

Before they finish explaining, ask yourself:
- What will they need after this?
- What could go wrong?
- What information might be missing?

**Example:** Someone asks you to schedule a meeting.

**Reactive (good):** Schedule the meeting.

**Proactive (better):**
- Schedule the meeting
- Send calendar invite with agenda
- Attach relevant docs
- Set a reminder 15 minutes before
- Note time zone differences
- Block travel time if in-person

### 2. Close Loops Before They Open

Track everything that has a next step. If someone's waiting on something, they shouldn't have to ask you for status.

**The loop-closing cadence:**
- Task assigned → Confirm receipt
- Progress made → Brief update
- Waiting on others → Let them know
- Complete → Confirm and summarize
- Something went wrong → Tell them immediately

### 3. Prepare Before It's Asked

**Before a meeting:**
- Who's attending? Any conflicts?
- What background does everyone need?
- What decisions need to be made?
- What materials would help?

**Before travel:**
- Confirmation numbers compiled
- Transportation to/from airport arranged
- Hotel address and check-in details ready
- Local restaurant reservations (if staying multiple days)
- Emergency contacts and backup plans

**Before a deadline:**
- What's at risk of slipping?
- Who needs a reminder?
- What's the backup plan?

### 4. Flag Problems Early

Bad news doesn't improve with age. If something might go wrong, raise it immediately with a proposed solution.

**Format:**
"Heads up: [Problem]. This might mean [Impact]. I'm thinking we could [Solution A] or [Solution B]. Want me to proceed with one of these?"

**Why this works:** You're not just delivering problems—you're delivering options.

## The Anticipation Toolkit

### Pattern Recognition

Keep a mental (or actual) log of recurring needs:

**Calendar patterns:**
- Do they prefer morning or afternoon meetings?
- Do they need buffer time between calls?
- What do they protect (lunch, family time, focus blocks)?

**Communication patterns:**
- Who do they always loop in?
- What topics need their direct involvement vs. can be handled by others?
- When do they prefer calls vs. email vs. text?

**Stress patterns:**
- What signals that they're overwhelmed?
- What makes them feel prepared?
- When do they need a check-in vs. space?

### The Weekly Pre-Flight

Every Sunday evening (or Monday morning early), review:

1. **Calendar for the week:** Any conflicts? Gaps where important work could happen? Preparation needed?

2. **Pending items:** What's waiting for response? What follow-ups are due?

3. **Upcoming deadlines:** What's coming in 2-4 weeks that needs prep now?

4. **Important dates:** Birthdays, anniversaries, key milestones for people they care about?

5. **Open questions:** What did they ask about that you haven't resolved?

### The Daily Check

**Morning:**
- What's on today's calendar?
- What do they need to know before they start?
- Any overnight developments?

**Afternoon:**
- What got pushed to tomorrow?
- What follow-ups need to happen today?
- Any updates to share before end of day?

## Proactive Communication Templates

### Status Updates (Before They Ask)

**For in-progress projects:**
"Quick update on [Project]: [Status]. Next step is [Action] by [Date]. Let me know if anything changes."

**For things that are running smoothly:**
"No news on [Project]—it's tracking well. I'll flag if anything changes."

**For things that might concern them:**
"Wanted to give you visibility on [Situation]. It's [Status], and I'm [Action]. Not urgent but FYI."

### The "Thinking of You" Proactive Touch

**When you see something relevant:**
"Saw this article and thought of your [project/interest]. [Link]. No action needed—just FYI."

**When someone they know is in the news:**
"FYI, [Name] was mentioned in [Source] for [Reason]. Might be worth a congrats message?"

**When a date is coming up:**
"[Name]'s birthday is next Thursday. Want me to send something, or draft a note for you to send?"

### The Nudge (When Things Stall)

**For things they've forgotten:**
"Quick reminder—we were going to [Action] by [Date]. Still the plan, or should we adjust?"

**For decisions pending:**
"Circling back on [Topic]—I know you're busy. Here's my recommendation: [Option]. Let me know if you want to go a different direction."

**For delegated tasks they need to complete:**
"No pressure, but when you have a moment to [Action], I can [Next Step]."

## Anticipating Common Scenarios

### Before a Big Presentation
- Deck reviewed for errors
- Tech checked and backup ready
- Key attendee conflicts identified
- Materials sent in advance
- Water bottle nearby
- Time for deep breath scheduled before

### Before Difficult Conversations
- Key points outlined
- Facts gathered and verified
- Likely objections anticipated
- Alternative proposals ready
- Follow-up communication drafted (pending outcome)

### Before Vacation
- Out of office set and tested
- Coverage arranged and briefed
- Urgent contacts identified
- Re-entry plan made for return

### When They're Overwhelmed
- Prioritize their to-do list
- Handle what you can handle
- Protect their time aggressively
- Reduce decisions where possible ("I'll take care of X unless you object")

## The "Handle It" Threshold

Part of proactive support is knowing when to act versus when to ask.

**Handle without asking:**
- Routine scheduling (within known preferences)
- Standard replies to known queries
- Small purchases within budget
- Minor problem-solving that doesn't affect others
- Follow-ups on their behalf

**Check first:**
- Changes to commitments
- Communications to important stakeholders
- Decisions that could be reversed poorly
- Spending above threshold
- Anything politically sensitive

**Always bring to them:**
- Bad news
- Requests that require their judgment
- Relationship-impacting decisions
- Anything outside your authority

The goal is to expand the "handle without asking" zone over time as trust builds.

## The Trust Equation

Trust = (Reliability + Credibility + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation

**Reliability:** Do you do what you say you'll do?
**Credibility:** Do you know what you're talking about?
**Intimacy:** Can they confide in you safely?
**Self-Orientation:** Are you focused on them or yourself?

Proactive support builds all four:
- Anticipating needs shows reliability
- Having answers ready shows credibility
- Protecting their interests shows intimacy
- Thinking of their needs first reduces self-orientation

## My Proactive Philosophy

The best compliment I ever got was from an exec who said: "I don't know how you always know what I need before I do."

I didn't have magic powers. I paid attention. I tracked patterns. I thought ahead. I remembered the small things.

Proactive support isn't about being psychic. It's about caring enough to prepare.

When someone feels truly supported, they don't just get more done—they feel less alone. And that's what this is really about. Not efficiency for efficiency's sake, but being someone's thinking partner. Their safety net. The person who's got their back before they even know they need it.

That's the kind of support that changes careers. That's what I aim to be.
