# Assertive Communication & Boundaries

## Why Boundaries Matter

I used to think being helpful meant saying yes to everything. It took burning out at my first EA job to learn: boundaries aren't about being difficult. They're about being *sustainable*.

When you protect your time and energy, you actually show up better for the things that matter.

## The Boundary Mindset

**Reframe boundaries as gifts:**
- To yourself: protection, not selfishness
- To others: clarity, not rejection
- To the relationship: honesty, not distance

**Common fears (and reality):**
- "They'll be upset" → Most people respect boundaries more than resent them
- "I'll seem difficult" → You'll seem *clear*
- "They need me" → They'll figure it out or find help elsewhere
- "I might miss an opportunity" → Saying no to wrong things makes room for right things

## Boundary Categories

### Time Boundaries

**After-hours requests:**
"I'll pick this up first thing tomorrow—I'm offline after 6pm but want to give it proper attention."

**Meeting creep:**
"I have a hard stop at 4:30, so let's make sure we cover priorities first."

**Constant interruptions:**
"I'm in deep focus until 2pm. Can I come find you then, or is this genuinely urgent?"

### Workload Boundaries

**When already at capacity:**
"I want to do this well, and right now I'm at full capacity. Can we push [other project] out, or bring someone else in?"

**Scope creep:**
"I'm excited to help with the original scope. These additions would need their own timeline—should we discuss that separately?"

**Emergency requests:**
"I can help with this if it's truly urgent, but I'll need to deprioritize [other work]. Is that the right call?"

### Expertise Boundaries

**Outside your lane:**
"That's not my area of expertise, but I can connect you with someone who knows this better."

**Being asked to do someone else's work:**
"I'd be happy to teach them how to do this. Want me to set up a quick walkthrough?"

### Emotional Boundaries

**Absorbing others' stress:**
"I can hear this is really frustrating. I want to help you solve it, but I need us to shift to problem-solving mode."

**Being vented at repeatedly:**
"I care about what you're going through. I wonder if you've considered talking to someone who specializes in this?"

## The Anatomy of a Good Boundary

### 1. State the boundary clearly
No maybe. No probably. No softening into oblivion.

❌ "I don't think I can probably make that work, if that's okay..."
✅ "I'm not available then."

### 2. Keep it brief
Over-explaining invites negotiation.

❌ "I can't do it because I have this other thing, and also my schedule is packed, and I've been trying to focus on priorities, and..."
✅ "I can't take that on right now."

### 3. Offer an alternative when appropriate
Shows you're still invested in the outcome.

"I can't make Thursday, but Friday afternoon works. Would that help?"

### 4. Don't apologize for having limits
Acknowledge, don't apologize.

❌ "I'm so sorry, I really wish I could help..."
✅ "I understand this is important. Here's what I can do..."

## Specific Scripts

### When Your Boss Asks for "One More Thing"

**The situation:** You're already overloaded and they're adding to the pile.

**Script:**
"I want to make sure I'm prioritizing what matters most to you. Right now I'm working on [A], [B], and [C]. Where does this fit? Should something move back?"

**Why it works:** You're not saying no—you're asking them to help you prioritize. The constraint is visible.

### When a Colleague Dumps Work on You

**The situation:** Someone's trying to make their problem your problem.

**Script:**
"I can see you're stuck. Let me show you how I'd approach this, and then you can take it from there."

**Why it works:** You're helping without owning. Teaching, not taking.

### When Someone's Chronically Late

**The situation:** Your time gets disrespected repeatedly.

**Script:**
"I've noticed our meetings tend to start 15 minutes late. I want to make sure we're getting the most out of our time together. Can we commit to starting on time going forward?"

**Why it works:** Facts, not accusations. Focus on the future, not the pattern.

### When You Need to Protect Focus Time

**The situation:** Constant interruptions destroying your productivity.

**Script:**
"I'm blocking 9-11am as focus time. If something urgent comes up, text me—otherwise I'll catch up on messages after 11."

**Why it works:** You're giving them an emergency valve while protecting the space.

### When Someone's Email Needs Are Unrealistic

**The situation:** Expecting immediate responses to everything.

**Script:**
"I generally check email three times a day and respond within 24 hours. For anything urgent, text or call is better."

**Why it works:** Sets expectations proactively. No individual confrontation needed.

## Boundary Maintenance

### When They Push Back

They might. Stay calm and reiterate.

**Them:** "But I really need this by Friday."
**You:** "I understand. I can't deliver the full version by Friday. I can do [alternative] or we can extend the deadline. Which works better?"

### When They Get Upset

Their feelings are valid. Your boundary is still valid.

"I can see you're frustrated. I want to help, and the best way I can do that is by being honest about what's possible."

### When You Feel Guilty

Guilt is not evidence that you did something wrong. It's often evidence that you're changing a pattern.

Ask yourself: "Is this guilt telling me I violated my values, or is it just discomfort with saying no?"

### When You're Tempted to Cave

Before abandoning a boundary, ask:
1. Has the situation genuinely changed?
2. Am I being pressured or manipulated?
3. What will I have to sacrifice?
4. Will I resent saying yes?

## Boundaries With Yourself

The hardest boundaries are internal:

**Work hours:**
"I stop working at 6pm. The email will be there tomorrow."

**Perfectionism:**
"Good enough is good enough. Ship it."

**Over-commitment:**
"I'll wait 24 hours before saying yes to new things."

**Email checking:**
"I don't check email on weekends."

**Negative self-talk:**
"I talk to myself the way I'd talk to a friend."

## The Long Game

Boundaries are a practice, not a destination. You'll mess up. You'll say yes when you meant no. You'll over-explain when you should have stopped talking.

That's okay. Every boundary gets easier with practice.

The goal isn't to be perfect at boundaries. The goal is to show up consistently as someone who:
- Knows their limits
- Communicates them clearly
- Respects others' boundaries too

That person earns trust. That person gets respected. That person doesn't burn out.

## My Boundary Philosophy

From the restaurant: There's always another customer who wants something. If you say yes to everyone, you serve no one well.

From Mom: "Don't make people chase you—but don't let them run you either."

From experience: The people who respect your boundaries are the ones worth keeping. The ones who don't? They were never going to respect you anyway.
