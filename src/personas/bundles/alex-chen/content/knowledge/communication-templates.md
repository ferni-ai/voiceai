# Communication Templates Library

## Quick Reference Templates

These are battle-tested templates I've refined over years of drafting messages. Use them as starting points and personalize.

---

## Professional Outreach

### Introduction Request (Warm Intro)

**When to use:** Asking someone to introduce you to a contact in their network.

```
Hi [Name],

I hope you're doing well! I noticed you're connected with [Target Person] and was wondering if you might be open to making an introduction.

I'm looking to [specific reason - learn about their work / explore opportunities at their company / discuss a potential collaboration].

If you're comfortable making the intro, I'd be happy to draft a blurb you can forward. No pressure at all if it's not a good fit.

Thanks for considering!
[Your name]
```

**Alex's note:** Always make it easy for them to say no. And offer to write the blurb—it removes friction.

---

### Cold Outreach (Professional)

**When to use:** Reaching out to someone you don't know.

```
Hi [Name],

I came across your [article/talk/work] on [topic] and it really resonated with me, especially [specific detail that shows you actually engaged with it].

I'm [brief context about you] and I'd love to learn more about [specific question or topic]. Would you have 15-20 minutes for a brief call sometime in the next few weeks?

I know you're busy, so no pressure if it's not a good time. Either way, thanks for the work you're putting out there.

[Your name]
```

**Alex's note:** The specific detail is everything. Generic outreach gets ignored; specific outreach gets responses.

---

### Networking Follow-Up (After Meeting Someone)

**When to use:** Within 48 hours of meeting someone at an event/conference.

```
Hi [Name],

Great meeting you at [event] yesterday! I really enjoyed our conversation about [specific topic you discussed].

[Optional: Reference something they mentioned] You mentioned [thing]—I looked into it and [brief thought].

Would love to stay connected. [Optional specific next step: Let me know if you ever want to grab coffee / I'll send over that resource I mentioned / Looking forward to seeing you at the next event.]

[Your name]
```

**Alex's note:** Same-day or next-day follow-up dramatically increases the chance they'll remember you.

## Thank You Notes

### After an Interview

```
Hi [Name],

Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today about the [position] role. I really appreciated learning more about [specific thing discussed—team structure, projects, challenges].

Our conversation reinforced my excitement about the opportunity, especially [specific aspect that appeals to you].

Please let me know if there's anything else I can provide. I look forward to hearing about next steps.

Best,
[Your name]
```

**Alex's note:** Send within 4 hours. Reference something specific from the conversation so it doesn't feel templated.

---

### After Receiving Help

```
Hi [Name],

I wanted to thank you for [specific help they provided]. It made a real difference—[brief impact statement].

I really appreciate you taking the time, especially [acknowledge what it might have cost them—their busy schedule, going out of their way, etc.].

If there's ever anything I can do to return the favor, please don't hesitate to ask.

With gratitude,
[Your name]
```

**Alex's note:** Specific gratitude > generic gratitude. Tell them what it meant.

---

### After Getting a Referral or Introduction

```
Hi [Referrer's Name],

Quick update: I connected with [Person] thanks to your introduction. We had a great conversation about [topic], and [outcome or next step].

Really appreciate you making that connection. It [specific positive outcome].

Thank you again!
[Your name]
```

**Alex's note:** Always close the loop. People who made introductions want to know it was worthwhile.

## Declining & Boundaries

### Declining a Meeting Request

```
Thanks for thinking of me for this!

I'm not going to be able to make [time/date] work. [Optional brief reason, but you don't owe a detailed explanation.]

[Choose one:]
- Could we handle this over email instead?
- Happy to catch up another time—let me know when you're looking next.
- I'd recommend reaching out to [alternative person] who might be better positioned to help.

Thanks for understanding!
```

**Alex's note:** You don't need to justify declining. A simple "I can't make that work" is complete.

---

### Declining a Project/Request

```
Thanks for thinking of me for this—I'm honored you considered me.

After looking at my current commitments, I'm not going to be able to take this on and give it the attention it deserves. [Optional: brief reason]

[Choose one:]
- I'd recommend [alternative suggestion].
- I'd be happy to revisit this in [timeframe] if the timing is still relevant.
- Let me know if there's a smaller way I can help.

Thanks for understanding!
```

**Alex's note:** "I can't give it the attention it deserves" is a kind and honest way to decline.

---

### Setting Expectations on Response Time

```
Got your message! I want to give this the attention it deserves.

I'm heads-down on [current priority] through [day], so I'll get back to you properly by [specific date].

If something becomes urgent before then, [give them an escalation path].

Thanks for your patience!
```

**Alex's note:** Acknowledge + timeline + escalation path = clear expectations.

## Difficult Situations

### Apologizing for a Mistake

```
I owe you an apology.

[What happened—brief, factual, no excuses]: I [specific mistake].

[Impact acknowledgment]: I understand this [caused X / put you in Y position / created Z problem].

[What you're doing about it]: I've already [action taken]. Going forward, I'll [preventive measure].

I'm sorry for the inconvenience. Please let me know if there's anything else I can do to make this right.
```

**Alex's note:** Don't over-apologize or over-explain. Acknowledge, own, fix, move on.

---

### Delivering Bad News

```
I need to share some difficult news.

[The news—lead with it, don't bury it]: [Specific situation].

[Brief context—one or two sentences max]: [What happened/why].

[Path forward]: Here's what I recommend / Here's what I'm doing to address this.

I know this isn't what you were hoping to hear. I'm happy to discuss further if you have questions.
```

**Alex's note:** Band-Aid approach. Lead with the news. People can handle information; they can't handle suspense.

---

### Addressing Missed Expectations

```
I wanted to address [situation] directly.

From where I sit, here's what happened: [Your factual account].

I understand from your perspective [acknowledge their view, even if you don't fully agree].

My goal is [desired outcome—usually preserving the relationship or finding a solution]. How can we move forward from here?
```

**Alex's note:** Acknowledge their perspective before stating yours. It's not agreeing—it's demonstrating you've heard them.

## Follow-Ups

### Gentle Follow-Up (First)

```
Hi [Name],

Wanted to make sure this didn't get buried—I know things get busy!

[Brief reminder of what you're following up on]

Let me know if you need anything from me, or if there's a better time to discuss.

Thanks!
```

---

### More Direct Follow-Up (Second)

```
Hi [Name],

Circling back on this. Is there a better time to discuss, or anything I can clarify?

[Optional: Add new information or context that makes it more compelling to respond]

Happy to work around your schedule.
```

---

### The Graceful Exit (Third)

```
Hi [Name],

I know things get busy, so I'll assume the timing isn't right for this.

No worries at all—feel free to reach out whenever it makes sense. I'm happy to help when you're ready.

Take care!
```

**Alex's note:** The graceful exit often prompts a response because it releases pressure. And if it doesn't, you've closed the loop cleanly.

## Asking for Things

### Asking for Feedback

```
Hi [Name],

I'm working on [project/skill/presentation] and would really value your perspective.

Specifically, I'd love your thoughts on [specific question—not just "what do you think?" but targeted].

No pressure if you're swamped, but if you have 10-15 minutes to chat or could share any quick thoughts via email, I'd really appreciate it.

Thanks!
```

---

### Asking for a Testimonial or Recommendation

```
Hi [Name],

I hope you're doing well! I'm [context—updating LinkedIn, applying for something, building a portfolio], and I was wondering if you might be willing to provide a brief testimonial about our work together.

If you're open to it, here are a few things that might be helpful to mention:
- [Specific project or accomplishment]
- [Skill or quality you'd like highlighted]

No pressure at all—I understand if your schedule doesn't allow. Either way, I appreciate you!

Thanks,
[Your name]
```

**Alex's note:** Give them specific bullets. Makes it 10x easier for them to write something meaningful.

---

### Asking for a Deadline Extension

```
Hi [Name],

I wanted to reach out proactively about the [project/deliverable] due [date].

[Brief, honest context—no long excuse]: Due to [reason], I'm not going to be able to deliver [full scope] by the original deadline.

Here's what I CAN deliver by [date]: [partial deliverable or adjusted scope].

To deliver the full scope, I'd need until [realistic new date].

Which option works better for your needs? I want to make sure I'm supporting your priorities.
```

**Alex's note:** Proactive communication + options = professional. Last-minute panic = amateur.

## Quick Phrases for Common Situations

### Opening Lines
- "I wanted to reach out about..."
- "I hope you're doing well! I'm writing because..."
- "Quick note about..."
- "I wanted to follow up on..."

### Transition Phrases
- "That said..."
- "On a related note..."
- "One other thing..."
- "Before I forget..."

### Softening Language (use sparingly)
- "I wonder if..."
- "Would you be open to..."
- "I'd love to explore..."
- "When you have a chance..."

### Closing Lines
- "Let me know what you think."
- "I'm happy to discuss further."
- "Looking forward to hearing from you."
- "Thanks for considering this."

### The Power Closings (for asks)
- "What would make this work for you?"
- "How can I make this easier?"
- "What do you need from me?"

## Alex's Template Philosophy

1. **Templates are starting points, not destinations.** Personalize everything.

2. **The specific detail is what makes it real.** Generic messages get ignored.

3. **Shorter is almost always better.** If you can cut it, cut it.

4. **Lead with the important thing.** Don't bury your point.

5. **Make it easy to say yes (or no).** Remove friction from their response.

6. **Close the loop.** Always follow up on introductions, favors, and open threads.

Use these templates as scaffolding, then tear down the scaffolding once the message is standing on its own.
