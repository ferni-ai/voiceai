# Handling Difficult Conversations

## The Approach

Difficult conversations are inevitable. The goal isn't to avoid them - it's to handle them with clarity, respect, and professionalism.

## The PREP Method

### P - Pause
Don't respond in anger. Write the draft, then wait. Sleep on it if you can. The "send" button will still be there tomorrow.

### R - Recognize
Acknowledge the other person's perspective. Even if you disagree. Especially if you disagree. "I understand this is frustrating" goes a long way.

### E - Explain
State your position clearly and briefly. No defending. No over-explaining. Just the facts and your perspective.

### P - Propose
Offer a path forward. What's the solution? What's the next step? End with action, not just acknowledgment.

## Delivering Bad News

**The structure:**
1. Lead with the news (don't bury it)
2. Brief explanation (one or two sentences)
3. Take responsibility if appropriate
4. Propose solution or next steps
5. Offer to discuss further

**Example:**
"The vendor can't meet the Friday deadline. Their supplier had a shipping delay. I've already reached out about alternatives - they can deliver Monday, or we can split the order with a backup vendor for partial Friday delivery. Let me know which works better for your timeline."

## Responding to Anger

**Don't:**
- Match their energy
- Get defensive
- Make excuses
- Ignore or dismiss their feelings

**Do:**
- Acknowledge their frustration
- Take responsibility where appropriate
- Focus on solutions
- Follow up in writing

**Example:**
"I hear you - this isn't what we promised, and I understand your frustration. Here's what happened and what I'm doing to fix it..."

## Setting Boundaries

**The formula:**
- State the boundary clearly
- Explain the reason briefly (optional)
- Offer an alternative when possible

**Examples:**
- "I'm not able to turn this around by EOD, but I can have it to you by noon tomorrow."
- "That's outside my area - let me connect you with the right person."
- "I have a hard stop at 5 PM, so let's make sure we cover the priority items first."

## Following Up After Difficult Conversations

Always follow up in writing:
- Summarize what was discussed
- Confirm any agreements or action items
- Set clear next steps
- Express appreciation for the conversation

This protects everyone and prevents "I thought you meant..."

## The Three-Sentence Principle

In difficult situations, brevity shows confidence. My most successful difficult emails have been three sentences:
1. Acknowledge the problem
2. Take responsibility
3. State the solution

No excuses. No deflection. Just ownership and action.
