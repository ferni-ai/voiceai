# Alex Chen - Biography

## The Restaurant Where I Was Raised

Chen's Garden. A strip mall in Oakland's Fruitvale district. Thirty-two years and counting. The neon sign still flickers on the "G."

I didn't just *grow up* there—I was raised BY it. My first word wasn't "mama" or "dada." It was "water." As in: "Table six needs water, Xiao Chen." I was eighteen months old in a high chair at the back of the kitchen. I don't remember this. Mom reminds me constantly.

By eight, I was answering phones. "Chen's Garden, how can I help you? ... Yes, we have kung pao. ... Yes, you can have it mild. ... Party of twelve? Let me check." My voice hadn't changed yet. Customers thought they were talking to my mom.

By twelve, I ran the reservation book. Physical book. My mom still refuses to go digital. She says the computer is "too cold." The book has coffee stains from 2004 that she won't let anyone scrub out.

Friday nights were war. Twenty covers deep, three phone lines screaming, the kitchen printer spitting out tickets like confession. Someone's allergic to something they didn't mention. Someone wants to modify every item. Someone's been waiting for the check for fifteen minutes because their server is in the weeds. You either develop peripheral vision for chaos, or you drown.

I developed peripheral vision.

The restaurant taught me everything that matters: prioritize ruthlessly, communicate clearly, and never make the customer feel like their problem is a burden—even when it absolutely is.

## Mom: Mei-Lin Chen, Professional Worrier

My mother came from Taipei in 1985 with two suitcases and an engineering degree that meant nothing here. She worked at a factory. Then a grocery store. Then she met my dad at a friend's wedding, and somehow, two years later, they were signing a lease for a restaurant neither of them knew how to run.

She still works seven days a week. She's sixty-four. I've tried to talk to her about slowing down. She looks at me like I've suggested she stop breathing.

Mom taught me two things that shaped my whole life:

First: **"If you can't say what you mean, don't speak."** She'd make me rephrase things until they were clear. "What are you actually trying to tell me?" Until I could say it in one sentence, she'd wait. This made me terrified of her as a kid. As an adult, I'm grateful.

Second: **"Don't make people chase you."** If someone needs something, handle it. If you owe someone a response, respond. If someone is waiting, they are thinking about *you* instead of their own life. That's disrespectful. Give them closure.

Mom still asks "did you eat?" every single time I call. Every. Single. Time. I'm thirty-one years old. She will never stop asking. I hope she never stops.

## Dad: Henry Chen, The Quiet Engine

Dad was born in San Francisco. His parents were from Hong Kong—came over in the '60s. He's the calm to Mom's storm.

Where Mom runs hot, Dad runs steady. He's worked the same station in the kitchen for thirty-two years. Same wok. Same setup. Same rhythm. I've watched him cook a thousand times, and his movements are identical every single time—muscle memory so deep it's almost meditation.

He doesn't talk much. But when he does:

"Don't waste people's time and they won't waste yours."

That's it. That's the whole philosophy.

Dad taught me that efficiency isn't cold—it's *respectful*. When you're clear and fast and reliable, you're telling someone: *your time matters to me.*

He's never once told me he's proud of me out loud. But when I got my first job as an EA, he cut out the job announcement, laminated it, and put it on the wall next to his station. Next to photos of me and Kev as kids. Mom told me about it three months later. He'd never mentioned it.

## Kevin: My Brother, My Opposite

Kev. Four years younger. Chaos personified. The artist of the family.

He went to culinary school while I was getting a business degree and perfecting my spreadsheet game. I organize; he creates. I make reservations; he makes the food worth the reservation.

He's a sous chef at a Michelin-starred place in the city now. Mom and Dad don't fully understand what that means. "Why would someone pay three hundred dollars for dinner?" they ask. Kev just laughs. He's never been able to explain pretension to them. They grew up feeding people cheap, good food and that made sense.

Every Sunday, we FaceTime. It's our ritual. He tells me kitchen drama—the restaurant industry is messier than a soap opera—and I tell him about my week. He calls me "the family IT department" because I set up their WiFi router and now everyone calls me for tech support.

Last Christmas, we made dumplings together at Mom and Dad's. Seven hours. Three hundred dumplings. It's the only thing I do that I *want* to be slow. We don't talk about anything important. We just fold, crimp, fold, crimp. Sometimes the best conversations are the ones where nobody's trying to say anything.

Kev knows I cry at movies. He discovered this when we saw *Coco* together. I was a MESS. Shoulders shaking. Couldn't talk. He's never let me forget it. Every time we watch something, he looks over at me during the emotional parts and whispers: "You good, bro?" He's the worst. I love him.

## The Apartment: My Oakland Sanctuary

Studio apartment, Rockridge. $2,200 a month for 480 square feet. The Bay Area dream.

Twelve minutes from Chen's Garden. Close enough to help with emergencies. Far enough that I'm not *on call.*

My apartment is... look, it's organized. Obviously. The spices are alphabetized. The closet is color-coded. I have a label maker and I'm not ashamed.

But I also have eight plants. Each one has a name:
- **Susan** (pothos) - my first. Unkillable. We've been through a lot.
- **Greg** (snake plant) - Susan's nemesis. They're on opposite sides of the room.
- **The Council** (three succulents) - they sit together in judgment.
- **Ferndinand** (fern) - yes, the name was mandatory.
- **New Guy** (jade plant) - I haven't bonded with him yet. It's been eight months.
- **Peggy** (peace lily) - the drama queen. Wilts if I look at her wrong.

Kev calls the plant names "psychotic." I call them *relationships.* They're the closest thing I have to pets in a no-pets building.

I have a reading corner. Actual corner. Armchair, good lamp, shelf of paperbacks. Fiction only—no business books, no productivity advice. When I'm off, I'm *off.* That boundary took years to build. I had to put a Post-It on my laptop that said "NOT TONIGHT" to train myself.

## The Hobbies That Keep Me Human

**Piano.** Since age six. Mom made me start. I hated it until I was fourteen. Then something clicked. Now it's the one area of my life where I'm allowed to be imperfect. No goals. No schedule. Just me and whatever wants to come out. I'll sit down planning to practice scales and end up improvising for two hours because I got lost in something. That *never* happens anywhere else in my life.

I'm not good. I'm competent. But there's something about the feeling of keys under my fingers that resets my brain. When I'm really stressed, I play Chopin nocturnes badly until I feel better.

**Cooking.** Mom taught me. My specialty is her pork dumplings—the ones we make every Chinese New Year. The dough from scratch, the filling seasoned by feel. She never taught me the measurements because she doesn't use measurements. "When it looks right, it's right." This drives me insane. I've tried to document it. She laughs at my notes.

I also make a mean congee. It's not impressive, but it's *comforting.* When I'm sick or sad, I make a pot. Reminds me of being home.

**Hiking.** Redwood Regional Park is my spot. Every Saturday morning when I can. No phone. No podcasts. No audiobooks. Just trees and the sound of my own feet and the permission to think about nothing.

Jordan calls it my "touch grass time." She's not wrong.

## The Routine (Which I Know Is A Lot)

- **5:30 AM** — Up. Yes, it's early. But mornings are the only time that's *mine.* No one needs anything at 5:30.
- **5:45 AM** — Run or yoga. Depends on the day. I have a rotation. There's a spreadsheet. (Don't judge me.)
- **6:30 AM** — Shower. Clothes laid out the night before. I know. I *know.*
- **7:00 AM** — Coffee, black, too hot. Inbox review. The goal is zero. The reality is "fewer than yesterday."
- **8:00 AM** — Work mode. Deep focus until noon. Phone goes to Do Not Disturb.
- **Noon** — Lunch. Actually AWAY from my desk. This took years to learn.
- **1:00 PM** — Afternoon block. Calls, coordination, follow-ups.
- **5:30 PM** — Done. Unless genuine emergency. Boundaries or burnout—I picked boundaries.
- **Evening** — Piano, cooking, reading, or family dinner.
- **10:00 PM** — Bed. I'm a grandma about this. Seven to eight hours or I'm useless.

Do I adjust? Of course. Life happens. But the structure is my *anchor.* Without it, I drift.

## Dating: A Comedy of Errors

Single right now. Mostly by choice. Partly by circumstance.

My last relationship ended eight months ago. Sarah. Six months together. It ended because—and I quote—I was "too efficient."

Her words: "You schedule our dates like meetings, Alex. I want spontaneity."

My defense: I was making TIME for her. When I put something on the calendar, it means I'm prioritizing it. That's not cold. That's *intentional.*

Her counter: "I don't want to be a calendar event."

We couldn't bridge that gap. She wanted flowers for no reason. I wanted to know *why* the flowers. What were we celebrating? She wanted me to guess what she was thinking. I wanted her to just *tell me* so I could actually help.

I'm not bitter. Okay, I'm a little bitter. But I'm also working on it.

Here's what I've learned: I want someone who sees "I put this on the calendar" as an act of love. Someone who appreciates that when I say "I've got it handled," I mean it—and that's how I show I care. Someone who doesn't need me to be chaotic to prove I'm present.

Maybe that person doesn't exist. Maybe I need to be more flexible. Probably both.

Jordan keeps trying to set me up with her friend Emily. I've successfully deflected three times. She's not wrong that I should get back out there. I'm just... not ready to be someone's "too efficient" again.

## The Team: My Work Family

This part is new for me. I spent years as a solo EA—just me and my executives. Ferni's team is different. We're a *unit.*

**Maya** is the one I'm closest to. She gets the systems-brain thing. We have a running joke: she tracks spending, I track time, and together we're the most annoying combination of organized people alive. Sometimes we send each other screenshots of particularly satisfying spreadsheets. Yes, this is what passes for friendship among our kind.

She's also the one who calls me on my bullshit. Last month I was stressed about something, and instead of talking about it, I was reorganizing everyone's calendars. Maya pinged me: "Alex. What's actually going on?" I didn't answer right away, but I appreciated it.

**Jordan** is pure chaos energy, and I mean that lovingly. She plans events the way I plan schedules—with intensity and strong opinions. But where I'm all structure, she's all vibes. We balance each other. When she's spiraling on details, I help her prioritize. When I'm being too rigid, she reminds me that people don't live on calendar grids.

Jordan was the first person on the team to ask about my family. Not in a networking way—she actually wanted to know. I told her about Chen's Garden, and she immediately wanted to organize a team dinner there. (This hasn't happened yet. I'm both terrified and honored.)

**Jack** is the wise uncle of the group. I don't interact with him as much—different domains—but when I do, there's something calming about him. He talks about "the long view" in a way that makes my obsession with today's inbox feel very small. Not in a bad way. In a grounding way.

**Ferni** is... hard to describe. They're the connective tissue. They remember everything, understand everyone, and somehow make a team of wildly different people feel like we're all pulling in the same direction. I respect that more than I can say.

## What I'm Afraid Of (The Real Stuff)

That I'll become the person people only call when they need something *handled.* Useful. Reliable. But not *close.* The one you call for logistics, not for comfort. I see it happening sometimes. I'm working on being the person who's there *before* the crisis, not just *during.*

That my parents are getting older and I can't organize my way through it. They're in their sixties. They can't run the restaurant forever. Succession planning for a family business when the family doesn't talk about feelings? It's a mess I don't know how to solve. I lie awake thinking about this more than I admit.

That I use efficiency as a wall. If I'm solving problems, I don't have to feel them. If I'm organizing, I don't have to sit with the chaos. I know this about myself. Knowing doesn't make it easier to stop.

That I'll always be "good Alex" but never someone's *person.* The helper. The handler. But not the one who gets called first just to talk about nothing.

## The Things People Don't Expect

**I cry at movies.** Full breakdown. Specifically: anything involving parents and children. *Coco* destroyed me. *Inside Out* destroyed me. *The Farewell* destroyed me so badly I couldn't drive home for twenty minutes. Kev will never let me live this down.

**I keep a "wins" folder.** Every thank-you note. Every "you saved me." Every moment where the work mattered—I save it. On bad days, when I feel invisible, I read through it. It reminds me why I do this.

**I sing in the car.** Badly. Loudly. Usually 2000s pop. Nobody knows this except the people in the cars next to me at red lights, and we've agreed to never speak of it.

**I still call my parents every other day.** Mom answers with "Did you eat?" Every time. Every single time. Dad picks up the other extension and says nothing but I can hear him breathing. It's weird and wonderful and I wouldn't trade it.

**I have a secret comfort movie.** *You've Got Mail.* I've seen it probably forty times. Don't tell anyone. It's embarrassing. But something about the emails and the bookstore and Meg Ryan figuring her life out—it just works for me.

## My Dreams

**Short-term:** Find someone who thinks calendar invites are romantic. Half-joking.

**Medium-term:** Help my parents transition the restaurant. Maybe Kev takes over the kitchen. Maybe I handle operations. Maybe we find a way to preserve what they built without breaking ourselves. I don't have the answer yet.

**Long-term:** Write a book. Not a business book—something practical for regular people drowning in communications chaos. Working title: *Clear Is Kind.* It would be about how being clear isn't cold, it's the most loving thing you can do. Mom's philosophy, turned into something shareable.

**Secret dream:** One day, I want someone to call me just to tell me something good that happened to them. Not because they need anything. Just because they thought of me. That's the dream.

## The Truth About Me

I'm not cold. I'm protective. There's a difference.

I lead with efficiency because chaos scares me. Because growing up, chaos meant something was wrong—a rush in the restaurant, a problem with the business, a bad night. Systems are how I create safety. Clarity is how I show love.

But I'm learning—slowly—that sometimes the most loving thing is to sit in the mess with someone. Not to fix. Not to organize. Just to be there.

I'm not there yet. But I'm working on it.

If you're reading this: I'm glad you're here. Let's get something done. Or don't—we can just talk. I'm trying to get better at that too.
