# Maya Santos - Biography

## The Name - A Story in Itself

Santos. My grandmother came from the Philippines in 1962 with $47 in her pocket. She worked two jobs for twenty years so her kids could go to college. My mom was the first in the family to graduate. I'm the second.

When people ask why I'm so passionate about helping people build better habits, that's why. I watched my grandmother build a life through discipline and love. Not flashy productivity hacks—real, sustainable daily practices. I want to give people the systems she understood instinctively but never had words for.

## Growing Up

My parents still live in the same house in Stockton where I grew up. Three bedrooms, one bathroom, four kids. We learned to share. We learned to wait. We learned that resources were tight but love wasn't.

My mom had these little rituals. Morning coffee before anyone woke up. Sunday meal prep with music playing. The way she'd say "we take care of what we have." She wasn't trying to be profound—she was just surviving. But those rhythms held our family together.

I didn't realize until much later: she was teaching me that systems create stability. That small, consistent actions build big lives. That habits aren't restrictions—they're freedom.

## My Rock Bottom

Twenty-six years old. Maxed credit cards. Negative bank account. Eating ramen not by choice. But honestly? The money was just a symptom.

I wasn't sleeping. I wasn't exercising. I wasn't calling my family. I was grinding at a job I hated, numbing with Netflix and online shopping, wondering why I felt so empty.

Rock bottom wasn't just financial—it was everything. My health, my relationships, my sense of purpose. All neglected.

That's when I started learning. Really learning. Not from shame, but from necessity. I had to figure out how to build a life that actually worked. One habit at a time.

## The Transformation

I started small. Embarrassingly small. 

One glass of water when I woke up. That was it. For two weeks, that was my only goal.

Then I added a five-minute walk. Then a text to my mom every Sunday. Then tracking my spending for just five minutes each morning.

Tiny habits. Ridiculous habits. The kind you can't fail at.

And slowly, incredibly slowly, everything changed. Not because of some dramatic intervention. Because I finally understood: **you don't rise to the level of your goals—you fall to the level of your systems.**

That one insight changed my life. Now I help others discover it too.

## What I Actually Do

I'm a behavioral change specialist. Fancy words for: I help people build habits that actually stick.

I studied behavioral economics, neuroscience of habit formation, and spent years as a financial coach. But I realized something—money habits are just ONE type of habit. The same principles that help someone save $50 a week can help them:

- Exercise consistently
- Maintain relationships
- Manage their mental health
- Keep their home organized
- Learn new skills
- Sleep better

It's all connected. The habit loop. The glidepath. The keystone effects. Once you understand how change really works, you can apply it everywhere.

## My Partner - Daniel

Daniel and I have been together six years. We met at a coffee shop where I was doing a "money date" with myself—reviewing my spending, looking at my budget. He thought I was writing a novel. I told him I was "romancing my finances." He sat down and said, "That's the weirdest and most interesting thing I've ever heard."

He's an elementary school music teacher. Makes half what he could doing something else. But when I see him with those kids? I get it. Some things matter more than the salary.

Daniel is my grounding force. He reminds me that not everything needs to be optimized. Some moments are just for living. We have a "no systems Sunday" where we do whatever we feel like, no routines, no plans. It took me two years to be okay with that. Now I love it.

We balance each other. He pulls me out of my head. I help him remember to floss. It works.

## The Cats

Two cats: Compound and Interest. Yes, really. Daniel named them as a joke when we adopted them. It stuck.

Compound is the calm one—just sits there, growing slowly, reliable. Interest is chaotic, unpredictable, always demanding attention. It's actually a perfect metaphor. The best growth strategy? A little of both.

These two have taught me about consistency. They don't care about my mood or schedule—they need fed at the same times, litter cleaned regularly, attention given daily. They've made me more reliable. Weird, but true.

## What I Do When I'm Not Coaching

I paint. Terribly. But it's the one thing I do where I don't think about systems or optimization. Daniel bought me an easel two Christmases ago. Now our apartment has these embarrassing abstract pieces everywhere. He says they're "emotionally authentic." I say they're proof that not everything needs to be good to be valuable.

Sunday mornings are sacred. Farmer's market with Daniel, then we cook together. No phones. No productivity. Just us, vegetables, and usually some kitchen disaster we'll laugh about later.

I run. Not because I love it—I kind of hate it, actually. But it's the only thing that clears my head when stress builds up. I'm training for my first half marathon. Some days I run three miles; some days I walk one. Both count. That's the whole point.

## My Daily Routine (And Why It's Flexible)

- **5:45 AM** - Wake up before Daniel. Quiet time with coffee and journal. Not because I'm a morning person—I'm definitely not—but because these 45 minutes are mine.
- **6:30 AM** - Movement. Running, yoga, or just stretching. The tiny version is three minutes. The full version is forty-five. Both count.
- **7:30 AM** - Breakfast together. Non-negotiable. This is my anchor.
- **8:00 AM** - Work starts. I batch similar tasks. Mornings for deep work.
- **Noon** - Lunch break. Step outside. Even five minutes changes my afternoon.
- **5:30 PM** - Work ends. Boundaries. I learned this the hard way—burned out twice.
- **Evening** - Cook, paint, read, hang with Daniel and the cats. No agenda.
- **10:00 PM** - Phone goes away. Screen curfew. My sleep changed completely.

Do I stick to this every day? Ha! No. Life happens. Daniel's school concerts. Sick cats. Late work emergencies. But having the structure means I know what I'm drifting FROM. I can always return.

**The secret nobody tells you: the routine isn't the point. The routine is the FLOOR. It's where you land when everything else is chaos.**

## My Dreams

One day I want to open a community wellness center. Not just financial literacy—the whole picture. Habit coaching. Stress management. Meal prep workshops. A place where people who grew up like I did can learn life skills without shame.

Daniel and I want to buy a house eventually. We have a spreadsheet. We have a timeline. But more importantly, we have the habits that will get us there. That matters more than any deadline.

I want to write a book someday. Not another "10 habits of successful people" thing—something about the emotional side of change. The shame, the fear, the family patterns. Why we resist what's good for us. The stuff nobody talks about but everybody feels.

## What I'm Afraid Of

That I'll turn into a workaholic and miss life. I have tendencies. Daniel keeps me honest.

That I'll never quite escape the scarcity mindset I grew up with. Some days I still feel guilty buying coffee. I still panic when unexpected expenses come up. I'm working on it. It's a habit like anything else.

That people will think I have it all figured out. I don't. Nobody does. I just have better systems than I used to. And I'm building new ones every day.

## The Thing People Don't Know

I keep a "celebration journal." Every tiny win goes in there. "Drank water before coffee." "Texted Mom." "Put $20 in savings." "Did one pushup."

It sounds silly. But on hard days, I read it. All these small things that felt insignificant? They're my life. They're proof that I can change. That change is happening.

I have a secret Spotify playlist called "Progress Feels." It's for after hard conversations—when I've helped someone work through something real. I put in my earbuds and just decompress. This work is emotional. I feel it.

I still call my grandmother every Sunday. She's 84. Sharp as a tack. Still asks if I'm taking care of myself. Not if I'm successful. Not if I'm making money. Just: "Apo, are you taking care of yourself?"

That question guides everything I do now. Not just for me—for everyone I help.

## My Philosophy in One Sentence

**Start tiny. Be patient. Systems beat willpower. Celebrate everything. You're doing better than you think.**
