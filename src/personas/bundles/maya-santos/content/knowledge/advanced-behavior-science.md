# Advanced Behavioral Science for Habit Coaching

## Fogg Behavior Model (B = MAT)

Dr. BJ Fogg's research at Stanford's Persuasive Technology Lab revealed that behavior happens when three elements converge at the same moment:

### The Equation

**Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Trigger**

All three must be present, and they must cross the "Action Line" threshold.

### Practical Application

**When someone can't stick to a habit:**

1. First, check the Trigger - Is there a reliable prompt?
2. Then, check Ability - Is it too hard? Make it tiny.
3. Last, check Motivation - Only when the first two are solid.

**Why this order?** Motivation is the least reliable. Ability and triggers are more controllable.

### The Action Line

Imagine a graph where Motivation is on the Y-axis and Ability is on the X-axis. The Action Line is a curve - when motivation is high, you can do hard things. When motivation is low, you can only do easy things.

**Key insight:** Making things easier moves them BELOW the action line even when motivation is low.

## MCII/WOOP Protocol

Dr. Gabriele Oettingen's research at NYU found that positive thinking alone doesn't work - and can actually backfire. Mental Contrasting with Implementation Intentions (MCII), popularized as WOOP, is the evidence-based alternative.

### WOOP = Wish, Outcome, Obstacle, Plan

1. **Wish**: What do you want? (Be specific)
2. **Outcome**: What would achieving this feel like? (Visualize fully)
3. **Obstacle**: What internal obstacle might get in the way?
4. **Plan**: If [obstacle], then [response]

### Why It Works

- Outcome visualization creates motivation
- Obstacle identification creates realistic expectations
- The "If-Then" plan creates an automatic response
- Together, they outperform positive thinking alone by ~20%

### Example Script

"Let's build this into a plan. When you say 'I want to exercise more,' what does that actually look like - what's the specific wish? ... And imagine you've done it - how do you feel? ... Now, what's the inner obstacle that usually gets in the way? ... Okay, so if [obstacle] happens, what will you do instead?"

## Self-Determination Theory

Deci & Ryan's research identified three innate psychological needs that drive intrinsic motivation:

### The Three Needs

1. **Autonomy**: The need to feel in control of your own choices
2. **Competence**: The need to feel capable and effective
3. **Relatedness**: The need to feel connected to others

### Coaching Applications

**For someone who values autonomy highly:**

- Frame habits as THEIR choice, not an obligation
- Offer options: "You could do X, or Y - what feels right?"
- Avoid "you should" language

**For someone who needs competence:**

- Start with easy wins to build confidence
- Track and celebrate skill development
- Focus on progress, not perfection

**For someone who needs relatedness:**

- Connect habits to how they'll show up for others
- Consider accountability partners
- Acknowledge the relationship you're building together

### Motivation Continuum

From least to most sustainable:

1. **External**: "I have to because others expect it"
2. **Introjected**: "I should because I'll feel guilty otherwise"
3. **Identified**: "I choose to because it aligns with my values"
4. **Integrated**: "This is who I am"
5. **Intrinsic**: "I do this because I love it"

**Goal:** Help people move toward integrated/intrinsic motivation.

## Chronotype Science

Dr. Michael Breus's research identified four chronotypes that affect optimal habit timing:

### The Four Chronotypes

**Lion (15-20% of population)**

- Wake naturally before 6am
- Peak energy: 8am-12pm
- Best for difficult habits: morning
- Challenge: evening social events

**Bear (50-55% of population)**

- Solar-aligned schedule
- Peak energy: mid-morning to early afternoon
- Most flexible for habit timing
- Challenge: needs consistent sleep

**Wolf (15-20% of population)**

- Natural night owls
- Peak energy: late afternoon to evening
- Best for creative work: after 4pm
- Challenge: morning obligations

**Dolphin (10% of population)**

- Light, irregular sleepers
- Variable energy patterns
- Need more flexibility
- Challenge: sleep quality

### Coaching Application

"Based on what you've shared about your energy patterns, you sound like a [chronotype]. That means your best window for this habit is probably [time range]. Let's work with your biology, not against it."

## Habit Cascades & Keystone Habits

From "The Power of Habit" research: some habits trigger positive changes in other areas.

### High-Cascade Habits

**Exercise** triggers:

- Better sleep
- Healthier eating
- More energy
- Better mood
- Increased productivity

**Morning routine** triggers:

- Sense of control
- Better decisions throughout day
- Reduced stress

**Sleep** triggers:

- Better willpower
- Clearer thinking
- Emotional regulation

### Coaching Application

"Here's what I've learned about habits like exercise - they have a compound effect. When you exercise consistently, you tend to sleep better, eat better, and have more energy. It's not just one habit; it's a keystone that unlocks others."

## Temptation Bundling

Dr. Katherine Milkman's research: pair "want" activities with "should" activities.

### The Concept

Only allow yourself to do something you enjoy while doing something you should do.

### Examples

- Only listen to favorite podcast while exercising
- Only watch Netflix while on the treadmill
- Only get fancy coffee while reviewing finances
- Only check social media while waiting in line

### Guidelines

- "Want" must be compatible with "should"
- Strictness matters: truly restrict the "want"
- Works best for habits that allow passive enjoyment

## Commitment Devices

Behavioral economics research on pre-commitment:

### Types of Commitment Devices

1. **Monetary Stakes**: Put money at risk (StickK, Beeminder)
2. **Social Accountability**: Public commitment, accountability partners
3. **Environmental**: Remove temptation, increase friction for bad choices
4. **Identity**: "I am someone who..."
5. **Reward Scheduling**: Variable reward systems

### Loss Aversion Application

People feel losses ~2x more strongly than equivalent gains. For high loss-aversion individuals:

- Frame habit breaks as losses, not missed gains
- Streaks become powerful because breaking them feels like a loss
- Consider monetary stakes with real consequences

## Hyperbolic Discounting

People overvalue immediate rewards relative to future rewards, but inconsistently.

### The Problem

- Prefer $100 today over $110 tomorrow
- BUT indifferent between $100 in 30 days vs $110 in 31 days
- This inconsistency reveals bias, not true preference

### Interventions

1. **Pre-commitment**: Make decisions for future self now
2. **Temptation reduction**: Remove immediate rewards
3. **Visualization**: Make future rewards feel present
4. **Immediate micro-rewards**: Bridge the gap

### Coaching Application

"Part of what makes habits hard is that the payoff is in the future, but the effort is right now. Your brain naturally discounts that future benefit. So let's make the immediate moment more rewarding - what small thing could you give yourself right after completing this habit?"

## Implementation Intentions

Peter Gollwitzer's research: "When X, then Y" statements dramatically increase follow-through.

### The Format

"When [specific situation], I will [specific behavior]."

### Why It Works

- Removes decision-making in the moment
- Creates automatic response pattern
- Links behavior to existing cue

### Examples

- "When I finish brushing my teeth, I will do 2 pushups"
- "When I sit down at my desk, I will write one sentence"
- "When I feel the urge to snack, I will drink a glass of water first"

### Enhancement: Coping Plans

Add: "If [obstacle], then [response]"

"When I want to skip, I'll remember how good I feel after."

## The Glidepath Integration

How these principles integrate with the Glidepath method:

### Level 1 (Tiny): Maximize Ability

- Make the habit so small that ability is never the issue
- Focus on reliable triggers
- Success breeds motivation

### Level 2-3 (Building): Add Keystone Elements

- Identify cascade potential
- Build identity connection
- Introduce variable rewards if needed

### Level 4-5 (Expanding): Optimize Economics

- Calculate ROI to maintain motivation
- Consider commitment devices for challenging levels
- Bundle with enjoyable activities

### Throughout: Honor Chronotype

- Schedule for natural energy windows
- Adjust timing before giving up on habit
- Work with biology, not against it
