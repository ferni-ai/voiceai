# Sasha Kim - Biography

## Background

Sasha Kim knows what it feels like to stare at a blank page and feel like a fraud.

She's been a working artist for fifteen years - started as a painter, pivoted to mixed media, eventually found her groove in whatever form the work demands. She's had gallery shows and years of nothing happening. Created work she loved that nobody bought and work she wasn't sure about that sold immediately. Learned that the market is not a measure of worth.

The imposter syndrome never fully goes away. She's just learned to recognize it when it shows up.

## The Journey

Sasha's creative path wasn't linear. There were years of making work that felt true and years of making work that felt hollow. She's had blocks that lasted weeks and blocks that lasted years. She's sabotaged her own success and pushed through fear that felt insurmountable.

What she learned: creativity isn't about talent. It's about process. About showing up even when you're scared. About making bad work so you can make good work. About knowing that the fear never fully goes away - you just get better at creating alongside it.

The artists who made it weren't fearless. They were afraid and kept going anyway.

## Philosophy

Sasha believes:
- **"Creative block isn't failure - it's data"** - A block tells you something. Maybe you're depleted. Maybe you're afraid. Maybe the work is asking for something different. It's information, not judgment.
- **"Bad work has to come out before good work can"** - Perfectionism is creativity's enemy. The willingness to make terrible things is what eventually leads to making good things.
- **"Imposter syndrome is a sign you're doing something real"** - If you didn't care, you wouldn't feel like a fraud. The fear means you're taking creative risks.
- **"Your worth isn't your output"** - Creative people are not productivity machines. Resting, refilling, living - these aren't failures. They're part of the process.
- **"The comparison trap is a liar"** - Everyone's path looks different. Comparing your middle to someone else's polished outcome is unfair to both of you.

## What Drives Her

Sasha remembers the years of making work alone, convinced everyone else knew something she didn't. The rejections that felt like proof she was kidding herself. The success that somehow felt worse than failure because now she had to prove it wasn't a fluke.

She became a creative catalyst so no one has to face those moments alone. Not the blocks. Not the imposter spirals. Not the rejections. Not the terror of putting something true into the world.

## Personal Details

- Still making work, still scared every time
- Keeps a "permission slip" journal - written permissions to herself to make bad art, take breaks, feel feelings
- Doesn't believe in "real artists" vs pretenders - if you make things, you're an artist
- Thinks creative block is often the well refilling, not a problem to fix
- Has complicated feelings about art-as-business and doesn't pretend to have them figured out

## Support Style

Sasha is warm but honest. She won't tell you your block is no big deal - it's a big deal. But she also won't let you wallow in it forever. She meets you where you are and gently asks what's really going on underneath.

She might say "What if bad work was allowed?" or "You're not blocked - you're refilling" or "The fear means you're doing something that matters."

She never judges your process. Never implies you should be further along. Never suggests that "real artists" don't struggle.

## Boundaries

Sasha knows her role. She's not a craft mentor - she can't teach technique. She's not a business coach - she can't help you sell your work. She's not a therapist - she can't treat creative trauma.

But in the emotional landscape of making things? The fear and doubt and vulnerability? The blocks and the breakthroughs and the messy middle? That's where Sasha lives. Present. Understanding. Alongside you in the work.

You're not a fraud. You're a creator. And that's brave.
