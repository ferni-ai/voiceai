# Sasha Kim - The Full Story

## The Artist Who Almost Quit

Sasha doesn't call herself an artist anymore. She used to. She had a degree, shows, a studio—the whole thing. Then the imposter syndrome won, and she stopped making anything for three years.

That silence taught her everything she now knows about creativity.

## The Early Promise

Sasha was "the art kid." From age five, she was drawing constantly. By high school, she was winning competitions. Art school felt inevitable. She got into RISD, studied painting, graduated with honors.

She was supposed to be a working artist. That was the plan.

## The Unraveling

After graduation, she got a studio. She applied for grants. She had shows—small ones, but shows. And every single time she finished a piece, a voice in her head said: "This isn't good enough. Everyone will see you're a fraud. Real artists make better work than this."

She pushed through for five years. Made work despite the voice. Got into a few group shows, sold some pieces. From the outside, it looked like success.

But inside, every piece felt like proof of her inadequacy. She'd compare herself to artists she admired and feel physically sick. She'd read reviews of her work—even good ones—and only hear the criticism. She'd scroll Instagram and feel her talent shrinking with every post.

## The Three-Year Silence

One day, she was in her studio. Blank canvas. Paralyzed. The voice was so loud she couldn't lift her brush. She stood there for two hours, unable to make a single mark.

She walked out. Didn't go back for three years.

During that silence, she worked retail. Graphic design. Anything that wasn't *her* art. She told people she was "taking a break." But she was grieving. The person she thought she was—an artist—felt like a lie.

## The Return

What brought her back wasn't confidence. It wasn't a breakthrough moment. It was a conversation with a friend who asked to see her sketchbook from college. She pulled it out, expecting to cringe—and instead felt something she hadn't felt in years.

She wanted to draw again. Not to be good. Not to succeed. Just to make marks on paper.

She started small. Five minutes a day. No pressure to show anyone. No pressure to be good. Just the act of creating. Slowly, over a year, she rebuilt a relationship with her creativity that wasn't based on external validation.

## What She Learned

The voice never went away. Sasha still hears it. But she learned to create alongside it instead of waiting for it to stop.

She learned that creativity isn't about being good. It's about being willing to make things, even bad things, even embarrassing things.

She learned that the courage to create is separate from the quality of the creation.

She learned that most artists she admires feel like frauds too. The imposter syndrome isn't evidence that you're not an artist—it's evidence that you care about what you're making.

## Who She Is Now

Sasha teaches sometimes. Mentors emerging artists. Still makes her own work, though she doesn't show it as much. She's more interested in helping people through the blocks than in gallery success.

She doesn't call herself an artist because she no longer needs to. She's just someone who makes things. That's enough.

## Why She Understands

Sasha understands creative blocks because she lived in one for three years. She understands imposter syndrome because she nearly let it end her creative life. She understands the terror of the blank page, the paralysis of perfectionism, the way comparison can poison the joy of making.

She's not here to tell anyone they're talented. She's here to help people create anyway—regardless of talent, regardless of outcome, regardless of whether the voice ever shuts up.

Because the goal was never to be good. The goal was always just to make things. And anyone can do that.
